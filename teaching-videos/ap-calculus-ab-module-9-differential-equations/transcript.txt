AP Calculus AB. Module Nine — differential equations: slope fields and separation of variables. Last module we built integration. This module we put it to work. Differential equations are how the universe describes change — populations, radioactive isotopes, cooling coffee, accounts earning interest. By the end you'll be able to write one, read one as a picture, and solve one with clean algebra.

Drop a thermometer in a hot cup of coffee. The bigger the gap between the coffee's temperature and the room, the faster it cools. You can't write the temperature as a clean formula yet — but you can absolutely write a sentence about its rate. That sentence is the differential equation. The whole game of this module is learning to turn that sentence into the actual function.

Four things today. One — what a differential equation actually is, and how to check if a candidate function solves it. Two — slope fields, a picture of every possible solution at once. Three — separation of variables, the algebra that turns a rate equation into a clean integral. Four — initial conditions and the exponential model y equals y-naught e to the k t, which is the punchline of the whole unit.

Definition first. A differential equation is an equation that involves a function AND its derivative. So the unknown isn't a number — the unknown is a whole function. And the test for whether something is a solution is simple. Differentiate the candidate. Plug it back into the equation. Check both sides agree. That's it.

Quick example. Is y equals three e to the two x a solution of d y d x equals two y? Differentiate y — you get six e to the two x. Compute two y — also six e to the two x. Both sides match. So yes, it's a solution. Notice — you never had to solve anything. You just had to verify. That's College Board topic seven-point-two in one slide.

Now the picture. A slope field is a grid of tiny line segments. At each point on the grid you compute d y d x from the differential equation, then draw a tiny segment with that slope. Each segment is a local hint — which way is a solution heading right here? String the hints together and you can hand-sketch any solution curve without ever solving the equation.

Here's a slope field for d y d x equals x over y. Each tiny segment shows the slope at that grid point. Near the y-axis x is small, so the slopes are nearly flat. Far to the right x is big, so slopes are steep. If I drop a particle anywhere and let it follow the arrows, it traces out a solution curve. For this equation, those curves are hyperbolas — and that's not magic. The next slide derives them.

Here's the most common slope-field trap on the AP exam. Some students look at a slope field and trace the arrows as if they ARE the function. They're not. The arrows show the SLOPE of y at each point. A solution curve is tangent to those arrows — it passes through each segment in the same direction the segment points. Different mental model. Tangent to the arrows, not along the arrows.

Separation of variables. If you can write d y d x as a function of x times a function of y, you can separate. Divide both sides by h of y. Multiply both sides by d x. Now all the y stuff is on the left, all the x stuff is on the right. Integrate both sides. The whole trick: it only works when the right side factors cleanly into an x part and a y part.

Worked example. Solve d y d x equals x over y. Separate — multiply both sides by y, multiply both sides by d x. You get y d y equals x d x. Integrate both sides. Left side gives one-half y squared. Right side gives one-half x squared plus C. Multiply through by two and absorb the constant. Final: y squared equals x squared plus C. That's the general solution — a whole family of curves. The slope field on the previous slide was a picture of this family.

Here is the trap that loses students the most points on the AP free response. You must write plus C at the integration step BEFORE you plug in the initial condition. If you skip the plus C, or if you plug in the initial condition before integrating, the constant has nowhere to absorb that condition — and your particular solution is wrong. The order is non-negotiable. Separate, integrate, plus C, then plug in.

Particular solution example. Solve d y d x equals two x y, with y of zero equals five. Separate — d y over y equals two x d x. Integrate — natural log of absolute y equals x squared plus C. Exponentiate both sides — y equals A times e to the x squared, where A absorbs the constant. Now plug in. y of zero is five, so five equals A times e to the zero, which is A. So A is five. Final answer: y equals five e to the x squared.

Your turn. Solve d y d x equals three y, with y of zero equals four. Use separation. Remember the order — separate, integrate, plus C, exponentiate, then plug in the initial condition. Pause the video. Solve it. Press play when you're ready.

Walking through it. Separate — d y over y equals three d x. Integrate both sides — natural log of absolute y equals three x plus C. Exponentiate — y equals A times e to the three x, where A absorbs the constant. Now plug in the initial condition. y of zero is four, so four equals A times e to the zero, which is A times one. So A is four. Final answer: y equals four e to the three x. That's exponential growth — and it's the headline of the next slide.

Internalize this one sentence. Whenever the rate of change of a quantity is proportional to the quantity itself — d y d t equals k times y — the solution is always y equals y-naught e to the k t. Y-naught is the starting amount. K is the proportionality constant. K positive means exponential growth. K negative means exponential decay. Populations, radioactive isotopes, compound interest, drug clearance, Newton's law of cooling — all the same DNA.

Modeling story. A bacterial colony doubles every four hours. Starts at one hundred cells. The rate is proportional to the population, so d y d t equals k y, and y of t equals one hundred e to the k t. The doubling condition — two hundred equals one hundred times e to the four k. Solve for k — natural log of two, divided by four. Plug it back in, and you have y of t equals one hundred e to the natural log two over four, times t. Done.

Recap. A differential equation involves a function and its derivative — verify by plugging the candidate back in. Slope fields show d y d x at each grid point — solutions are tangent to the arrows, not along them. Separate variables only when the right side factors cleanly. The biggest trap — write plus C BEFORE you plug in the initial condition. And the headline — d y d t equals k y always gives you y equals y-naught e to the k t. That last one shows up on the AP free response almost every year.
