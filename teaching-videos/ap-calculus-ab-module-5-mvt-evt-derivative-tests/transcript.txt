AP Calculus AB. Module Five — the Mean Value Theorem, the Extreme Value Theorem, and the two derivative tests. This is where derivatives stop being a calculation and start being a way of thinking about functions. By the end of this lesson you'll have the four theorems that let you find every max, every min, and every shape change on a curve.

Picture this. You blow through a toll booth at noon, then hit the next one fifty miles away forty minutes later. The toll-system computes your average speed at seventy-five miles per hour — the speed limit is sixty-five. The ticket is mailed to your house. That ticket is legal, and the reason it's legal is the Mean Value Theorem. Somewhere between those two booths, your speedometer had to read exactly seventy-five.

We'll move in three layers. First, the two existence theorems — MVT and EVT — that tell you what must be true. Second, critical points and the First Derivative Test, the workhorses for finding extrema. Third, concavity and the Second Derivative Test, the shape story of a curve. Each layer is a question the AP exam will absolutely ask you.

Here's the Mean Value Theorem precisely. If f is continuous on the closed interval from a to b, and differentiable on the open interval from a to b, then there exists some c in the open interval where f prime of c equals f of b minus f of a, all over b minus a. In plain English — the instantaneous slope at c matches the average slope across the whole interval.

Geometrically, draw the secant line from the point a, f of a to the point b, f of b. The slope of that secant is the average rate of change. The Mean Value Theorem promises a tangent line, somewhere strictly inside, that is parallel to that secant. Take f of x equals x squared on the interval zero to two. The secant slope is two. Solve f prime of c equals two c equals two and you get c equals one.

The Extreme Value Theorem is even simpler to state. If f is continuous on a closed bounded interval from a to b, then f attains both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum somewhere on that interval. Drop continuity, or drop the closed-and-bounded condition, and you can lose the guarantee. Example — f of x equals one over x on the open interval zero to one has no absolute maximum, because the function blows up near zero.

A critical point of f is an interior point c where f prime of c equals zero, or where f prime of c does not exist. Every relative max and every relative min is a critical point — that's a fact. But the reverse is not true. Classic counterexample — f of x equals x cubed has f prime of zero equals zero, so zero is a critical point, but the function is just passing through. It is neither a max nor a min.

Monotonicity follows directly from the sign of the derivative. Where f prime is positive, f is increasing. Where f prime is negative, f is decreasing. The workflow is mechanical — find the critical points, plot them on a number line, test the sign of f prime in each interval, and you've got a sign chart that reads off increasing and decreasing intervals at a glance.

The First Derivative Test classifies a critical point by how f prime changes sign at it. Plus to minus means a relative maximum — function climbs then falls. Minus to plus means a relative minimum — function falls then climbs. No sign change means neither, the classic x cubed situation. For f of x equals x cubed minus three x, f prime equals three x squared minus three is zero at plus and minus one, and the sign chart gives a max at minus one, a min at one.

Your turn. Take f of x equals x cubed minus twelve x. Find every critical point, and classify each one using the First Derivative Test. Solve f prime equals zero, then check the sign of f prime on each side of every critical point. Pause the video, work it out on paper, press play when you're ready.

Walking through it. f prime of x equals three x squared minus twelve, which factors as three times x minus two times x plus two. So the critical points are x equals plus and minus two. Testing the sign of f prime — pick a number less than minus two, say minus three, you get positive. Between minus two and two, say zero, you get negative. Past two, say three, positive again. Plus to minus at minus two gives a relative max with value sixteen. Minus to plus at two gives a relative min with value negative sixteen.

Now for absolute extrema on a closed interval. The Candidates Test. Step one — list every critical point inside the interval. Step two — add the two endpoints, x equals a and x equals b. Step three — evaluate f at every candidate. The largest value is the absolute max, the smallest is the absolute min. EVT guarantees they exist, the Candidates Test tells you exactly where.

The second derivative tells you the curvature. When f double prime is positive, the curve is concave up — cup shape, slopes increasing. When f double prime is negative, concave down — cap shape, slopes decreasing. An inflection point is where f double prime actually changes sign, and the function is defined there. Just having f double prime equal zero is not enough — you need the sign change.

The Second Derivative Test is the shortcut. If f prime of c is zero and f double prime of c is positive, you have a relative minimum. If f double prime of c is negative, a relative maximum. But when f double prime of c equals zero, the test gives you nothing — it is inconclusive, and you must fall back to the First Derivative Test. Why? Look at f of x equals x to the fourth. f double prime of zero equals zero, and zero is in fact a minimum. Now look at f of x equals x cubed. f double prime of zero also equals zero, but zero is not an extremum at all. Same f double prime value, completely different behavior. That's why the test breaks when f double prime is zero.

The classic AP trap. A zero derivative does not by itself make an extremum. The wrong move — write f prime of zero equals zero for f of x equals x cubed, conclude it's a minimum, lose the point. The right move — check whether f prime actually changes sign at the critical point. For x cubed, f prime equals three x squared is never negative, so zero is a critical point but neither a max nor a min — it is an inflection point. Verify the sign change, or use the Second Derivative Test.

Six takeaways. One — MVT: continuous on the closed interval, differentiable on the open, gives a point where instantaneous slope equals average slope. Two — EVT: continuous on a closed bounded interval guarantees an absolute max and min exist. Three — critical point: f prime is zero or undefined, necessary but not sufficient for an extremum. Four — First Derivative Test classifies by sign change of f prime. Five — Candidates Test compares f at critical points and endpoints for absolute extrema. Six — Second Derivative Test: positive means min, negative means max, zero is inconclusive.
