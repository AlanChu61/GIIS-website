AP Calculus AB. Module Ten — average value and area between curves. Unit Eight A. Last module we built integrals and the Fundamental Theorem. This module we put them to work — averages, particle motion, and the shaded region between two curves. These are some of the most common free response setups on the AP exam.

A bug walks four meters east, then seven meters west. Where is it? Three meters west of where it started. How far did the bug actually walk? Eleven meters. Same trip — two different numbers. Calculus has to tell those numbers apart. One of them is net displacement, the other is total distance, and the AP exam will absolutely ask you for both in the same problem.

Three things today. One — the average value of a function on an interval, and the divide-by-b-minus-a trap. Two — particle motion: net displacement is one integral, total distance is a different integral, and you cannot mix them up. Three — area between two curves, including the messy case where they cross more than twice.

The average value of f on the interval from a to b is one over b minus a, times the definite integral of f from a to b. Read it twice. You DIVIDE by b minus a. The single most common mistake on this topic is multiplying instead. The factor out front is one over the width of the interval — same as taking an average of a list of numbers and dividing by how many you have.

Geometrically — average value is the height of the rectangle on the same base that has the same area as the region under the curve. Take f of x equals x squared on the interval zero to three. The integral is nine. Divide by the width, three. The average value is three. Drop a horizontal line at y equals three, and the rectangle above the base has area nine — the same area as under the parabola. That picture is what the formula means.

There's a Mean Value Theorem for Integrals that goes with this. If f is continuous on a closed interval, then somewhere strictly inside that interval, the function actually takes on its average value. So the horizontal line at y equals three from the last slide — the parabola crosses it somewhere between zero and three. Geometrically obvious. The AP exam will ask you to identify that crossing point.

Particle motion. Position is s of t. Velocity is the derivative, s prime of t. Acceleration is v prime of t. That's the forward chain — differentiate to step down. Now go the other way. Integrate. Integrate acceleration to get velocity. Integrate velocity to get change in position. That bidirectional chain is the whole Unit Eight setup.

Here is the comparison that costs students the most points on this entire course. Net displacement equals the integral of velocity from a to b. It can be negative — it tells you where the particle ended up relative to where it started. Total distance traveled equals the integral of the absolute value of velocity from a to b. It is never negative. You need both quantities, and the AP exam will ask for them in the same problem.

Worked example. Velocity equals t squared minus four, from t equals zero to three. Net displacement — integrate v straight through. Antiderivative t cubed over three minus four t. At three minus zero: nine minus twelve equals negative three. The particle ends three units to the left of where it started. Total distance — velocity is negative on zero to two and positive on two to three. Split at t equals two, take absolute value on each piece. Sixteen thirds plus seven thirds equals twenty-three thirds. Same trip. Different number.

More general — accumulation. If f of t is a rate, then the integral from a to b of f of t dee t is the total accumulated amount over that time. Water flowing into a tank at r of t gallons per minute — the integral of r is total gallons added. That's College Board topic eight point three. One important gotcha — the units of the integral are units of f times units of t, not just units of f.

Area between curves. Two functions on the same axes. The area trapped between them, from x equals a to x equals b, is the integral of top curve minus bottom curve, dee x. The orientation matters. Top minus bottom — always. Reverse the order and you get the negative answer. The magnitude might look right, but the AP exam marks the sign wrong, and you lose the setup point.

Quick example. Area between y equals x and y equals x squared from zero to one. First find where they meet — at zero and at one. Between them, the line y equals x sits above the parabola. So top minus bottom equals x minus x squared. Integrate from zero to one. Antiderivative is x squared over two minus x cubed over three. Plug in one: one half minus one third. One sixth. Done.

Sometimes the curves are easier to write as x equals something of y. Then switch — integrate in y. Now the formula is right curve minus left curve, dee y, from y equals c to y equals d. Example. x equals y and x equals y squared minus two. They meet at y equals negative one and y equals two. The line x equals y is on the right. Set up: integral of y minus the quantity y squared minus two, dee y, from negative one to two. Nine halves.

Your turn. Find the area enclosed between y equals x squared and y equals two x. Step one — find where they intersect. Step two — figure out which curve is on top in between. Step three — set up top minus bottom and integrate. Pause the video. Solve it. Press play when you're ready.

Walking through it. Set them equal — x squared equals two x — so x squared minus two x equals zero, factor x times x minus two. Intersections at x equals zero and x equals two. At a test point x equals one, the line gives two and the parabola gives one. Line is on top. Integrate two x minus x squared from zero to two. Antiderivative x squared minus x cubed over three. At two: four minus eight thirds equals four thirds. The enclosed area is four thirds.

The classic AP trap. Two curves that cross more than twice. The top and bottom swap each time they cross. If you just integrate one minus the other straight through from start to end, the positive and negative regions cancel and your answer is wrong. You have to split the integral at every crossing point. On each sub-interval, identify which curve is on top, set up top minus bottom, integrate, and add up the pieces.

The biggest trap of Unit Eight. Wrong way — student integrates velocity straight through from zero to T and reports the result as total distance. If velocity changed sign anywhere in there, that answer is wrong. Right way — find every zero of velocity, split the integral at each sign change, take absolute value on each piece, sum. Net displacement and total distance are two different quantities. The AP exam knows which one it asked for, and the wording is always clear if you slow down to read it.

Recap. Average value: one over b minus a, times the integral. You divide. Mean Value Theorem for Integrals — the function actually achieves its average somewhere inside. Particle motion: integral of v is net displacement, integral of absolute value of v is total distance. Area between curves: top minus bottom dee x, or right minus left dee y. When the curves cross more than twice, split at every crossing and take absolute value on each piece.
