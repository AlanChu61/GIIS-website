Welcome back to AP Psychology. Module Five — states of consciousness. Sleep, dreams, hypnosis, and drugs. By the end of today you'll know what your brain is actually doing while you're unconscious for a third of your life, and the AP-favorite traps inside dreams and drug mechanisms. Let's go.

Quick scenario. You pull an all-nighter for a test. You stay technically awake the whole time. The next morning you can't focus, your jokes don't land, you re-read the same paragraph four times, and you're weirdly emotional at lunch over a sandwich. What happened? You weren't just tired. Your brain skipped the maintenance cycle it runs every night — the one that consolidates memory, regulates mood, and clears out cellular junk. That cycle is consciousness shifting through stages. And once you know what those stages do, you understand why a missed night feels like a missed shower for your brain.

Four buckets today. One — levels of awareness, from fully conscious down to unconscious, and what Freud meant by preconscious. Two — the sleep stages, NREM one, two, three, and REM — and the famous ninety-minute cycle. Three — dreams and hypnosis, and the competing theories AP wants you to compare. Four — psychoactive drugs — agonist versus antagonist, the three big categories, and the words tolerance, dependence, and withdrawal. Buckle in.

Start at the top. Consciousness is your awareness right now — the words you're reading, the sounds in the room. Below that, the preconscious — Freud's term — is information you're not thinking about but could pull up easily. What did you eat yesterday? It just popped up. That was preconscious. Below that, the unconscious — for Freud, hidden drives and repressed memories shaping behavior. Modern psychology prefers the term nonconscious for processes the brain runs in the background — heart rate, breathing, balance. And then there are automatic processes — things you used to do consciously, like tying your shoes or driving home, that became automatic with practice. Free up the spotlight, hand the rest to autopilot. That's the trick the brain uses all day.

Now sleep. Every ninety minutes or so, your brain runs through a cycle. NREM stage one — light sleep, you might twitch, you're easy to wake. NREM stage two — sleep spindles on the EEG, you're definitely asleep. NREM stage three — slow-wave sleep, deep sleep, this is where the body repairs and growth hormone releases. Then it climbs back up and crosses over into REM — rapid eye movement. In REM, your brain looks awake on an EEG, your eyes dart around, your dreams are vivid and storylike — and your body is paralyzed. That paralysis is protective; without it, you'd act out your dreams. AP calls REM 'paradoxical sleep' — brain on, body off. One more crucial fact — REM gets longer each cycle. Early in the night you get more deep NREM. Toward morning, you get more REM. That's why your wildest dreams are right before you wake up.

Why do we sleep at all? Three theories AP wants you to know. Restorative — sleep repairs the body and brain. Fits the deep NREM stage. Consolidation, sometimes called memory theory — sleep moves information from short-term to long-term memory. Fits REM, when the hippocampus replays the day. Evolutionary — being still and quiet at night kept our ancestors safe from predators. None of these fully explains sleep on its own. Most researchers say it's all three at once.

Sleep disorders. Five to know. Insomnia — chronic trouble falling or staying asleep. Most common, often tied to stress or anxiety. Narcolepsy — sudden uncontrollable sleep attacks, often dropping straight into REM. Rare, dangerous when driving. Sleep apnea — breathing repeatedly stops during sleep, person partially wakes to breathe, never gets deep sleep, wakes up exhausted. Often goes undiagnosed for years. Somnambulism — sleepwalking — happens in NREM stage three, deep sleep, mostly in kids. They're not acting out a dream, they're stuck between stages. And REM sleep behavior disorder — the paralysis fails, the person physically acts out their dreams, sometimes injuring themselves or a partner. The neat AP detail — sleepwalking is NREM, dream-acting is REM. Don't confuse them.

Welcome back. Question one — REM. The brain is electrically active and looks awake on the EEG, which is why we call REM paradoxical sleep. The body is paralyzed because the brainstem switches off voluntary muscle signals — without that lock, you'd kick, swing, or run during dreams. Question two — sleepwalking happens in NREM stage three, deep slow-wave sleep. People expect it during dreams in REM, but in REM the body is paralyzed. So sleepwalkers aren't acting out a dream. They're partially awake during deep non-dream sleep. The vivid-dream-acting version is a separate disorder called REM sleep behavior disorder, when the paralysis itself fails. Two different stages. Two different mechanisms.

Dreams. Three theories — AP loves to compare them. Freud's psychoanalytic view — dreams have manifest content, what literally happened in the dream, and latent content, the hidden symbolic meaning. Largely abandoned in research, still cultural. Hobson's activation-synthesis theory — during REM, the brainstem fires random neural signals, and the cortex tries to weave them into a story. The story isn't meaningful; the brain just hates randomness. And the information-processing view — dreams are the brain sorting and consolidating the day's memories. Tomorrow's test? Probably going to dream about it. Bottom line — Freud says dreams hide meaning, Hobson says they're random signal getting narrated, information-processing says they're memory sorting in disguise.

Hypnosis. Two competing explanations. Hilgard's divided consciousness theory — hypnosis splits awareness, with a 'hidden observer' part of the mind aware of pain or events even when the conscious self isn't. Cool concept. Strong evidence in pain studies. The social influence theory — hypnosis isn't a special state, it's people playing a deeply suggested role, like a really immersive expectation. Both have evidence. AP wants you to be able to name both. The practical truth — hypnosis can reduce perceived pain, can help with focus and habit change, and can make people do mildly silly things they're already willing to do. It cannot make you do something against your core values, it cannot retrieve buried 'true' memories — that's a recipe for false memories — and it is not the magic mind control you see in movies.

Psychoactive drugs. First, the mechanism words. An agonist mimics or boosts a neurotransmitter — heroin acts as an agonist at endorphin receptors, which is why it kills pain. An antagonist blocks a neurotransmitter — naloxone, the overdose reversal drug, is an opioid antagonist; it kicks heroin off the receptor. Memorize: agonist mimics, antagonist blocks. Now three categories. Stimulants speed up the central nervous system — caffeine, nicotine, cocaine, methamphetamine. They raise heart rate, alertness, dopamine. Depressants slow it down — alcohol, opioids like heroin and oxycodone, and benzodiazepines like Xanax. They calm anxiety, slow breathing, and at high doses can be deadly. Hallucinogens distort perception and reality — LSD, psilocybin, MDMA, marijuana. Marijuana is technically its own category but AP usually groups it here. None of these are abstract. Caffeine is a stimulant. Alcohol is a depressant. That alone wins you points on the exam.

Three terms tied to all of this. Tolerance — the body gets used to a drug, so you need more to feel the same effect. The first sip of coffee in seventh grade hit hard. Three years of three coffees a day, you barely feel one. That's tolerance — the brain adjusted. Dependence — the body needs the drug to feel normal. Two flavors — physical dependence, the body has rewired itself, and psychological dependence, the cravings and habit. Withdrawal — the unpleasant symptoms when the drug is removed. Headaches when a heavy coffee drinker quits. Tremors and seizures when an alcoholic quits. Withdrawal severity is a clue to how strongly the brain had adapted. Tolerance, dependence, withdrawal — three different ideas, all pointing at the same thing — the brain trying to find a new normal around a chemical.

Recap. Awareness has levels — conscious, preconscious, unconscious, plus automatic processes. Sleep cycles every ninety minutes through NREM one, two, three, and REM. REM is paradoxical — brain awake, body paralyzed — and gets longer toward morning. Three theories of sleep — restorative, consolidation, evolutionary. Five disorders — insomnia, narcolepsy, sleep apnea, somnambulism in NREM, REM sleep behavior disorder. Three theories of dreams — Freud, activation-synthesis, information-processing. Two theories of hypnosis — divided consciousness, social influence. Drugs — agonist mimics, antagonist blocks, three categories — stimulants, depressants, hallucinogens. Tolerance, dependence, withdrawal.
