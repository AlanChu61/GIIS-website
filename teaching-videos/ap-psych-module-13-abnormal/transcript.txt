Here's something to sit with for a second. Roughly one in five adults will meet the criteria for a diagnosable mental disorder in any given year. Look around your classroom. That's the math. These are common, treatable conditions — not rare, not shameful, not made up. The line between what we call 'normal' and what we call 'a disorder' isn't as clean as people think. So psychologists use four questions to decide. We'll learn them today.

Three things today. One — how psychologists define abnormal in the first place. The four D's. Two — the DSM-5 framework and the major categories of disorders. Anxiety, OCD, trauma, mood, schizophrenia, personality, dissociative. Three — the diathesis-stress model, which explains why two people with the same risk can have totally different outcomes. By the end you'll be able to read a description of someone's symptoms and place them in the right category.

First — what makes a behavior abnormal? Psychologists use four D's. Deviance — the behavior is statistically rare or violates social norms. Distress — the person feels significant emotional pain. Dysfunction — the behavior interferes with daily life, work, school, relationships. Danger — there is risk of harm to self or others. Here's the key — none of these alone is enough. Being unusual isn't a disorder. Being sad isn't a disorder. We usually need multiple D's together before clinicians will diagnose. This nuance is exactly the kind of thing AP loves to test.

The standard tool clinicians use in the United States is the DSM-5 — the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, fifth edition. It lists the symptom criteria for every recognized disorder. Internationally, most countries use the I-C-D eleven instead, which is broadly similar. Here's a critical AP point. The DSM is descriptive — it tells you what a disorder looks like — but it is not explanatory. It does not tell you what causes the disorder, or how to treat it. Two clinicians using the DSM should land on the same diagnosis, but the why and the how come from psychological theory.

Now the major categories. First — anxiety disorders. Generalized anxiety disorder, or GAD, is chronic, free-floating worry, not tied to one specific thing. Panic disorder is sudden, intense attacks — racing heart, can't breathe — often with no clear trigger. Phobias are intense fears of a specific object or situation, like heights or social settings. Closely related, but its own category in the DSM-5, is obsessive-compulsive disorder, OCD. Obsessions are intrusive thoughts. Compulsions are repetitive behaviors done to relieve those thoughts — washing, checking, counting. And trauma- and stressor-related disorders include PTSD — re-experiencing a traumatic event through flashbacks, nightmares, and hyperarousal.

Mood disorders. Major depressive disorder is at least two weeks of persistent sadness, loss of interest, low energy, sleep and appetite changes, and difficulty concentrating. Persistent depressive disorder is a milder but longer version — at least two years of low mood. Bipolar disorders involve mood swinging between depression and mania. Bipolar one includes full manic episodes — racing thoughts, no need for sleep, risky behavior. Bipolar two includes hypomania — a milder elevated state — with depressive episodes. These are treatable. Therapy and medication change lives. That part matters.

Welcome back. Three of the four D's are clearly present. Distress — she feels deep sadness. Dysfunction — she can't attend class or function day-to-day. Deviance — this is a significant departure from her usual baseline. Danger is not present, which is fine — you don't need all four. The DSM category is mood disorders, specifically major depressive disorder. Two weeks plus of persistent sadness, loss of interest, and dysfunction is the textbook profile. Most importantly — this is treatable. Therapy plus medication has strong evidence. Real recovery is the expected outcome, not the exception.

Schizophrenia. A serious disorder marked by disrupted thinking, perception, and emotion. AP wants you to know two clusters of symptoms. Positive symptoms — things added that shouldn't be there. Hallucinations, usually auditory voices. Delusions, fixed false beliefs. Disorganized speech. Negative symptoms — things missing that should be there. Flat affect — reduced emotional expression. Avolition — lack of motivation. Social withdrawal. The leading biological theory is the dopamine hypothesis — overactivity of dopamine pathways in the brain contributes to positive symptoms, which is why most antipsychotic medications block dopamine receptors. Critical language note — say 'a person with schizophrenia,' not 'a schizophrenic.' The disorder is not the person.

Personality disorders. These are enduring, inflexible patterns of thought and behavior that cause distress or dysfunction. The DSM groups them into three clusters. Cluster A — odd or eccentric. Paranoid, schizoid, schizotypal. Cluster B — dramatic, emotional, erratic. Borderline, narcissistic, antisocial, histrionic. Cluster C — anxious or fearful. Avoidant, dependent, obsessive-compulsive personality disorder, OCPD. One quick clarification — OCPD is not OCD. OCPD is a rigid personality style focused on order and control. OCD involves intrusive thoughts and compulsions. Different things, similar names. AP loves that confusion. Last category to mention — dissociative disorders, including dissociative amnesia and the rare and controversial dissociative identity disorder, DID.

One more big idea. The diathesis-stress model. It explains why two people with the same family history of, say, depression can end up with completely different outcomes. Diathesis is a predisposition — genetic, biological, or psychological vulnerability. Stress is a triggering life event. The disorder shows up when both are present. High vulnerability plus heavy stress equals onset. Low vulnerability plus the same stress, no onset. High vulnerability plus low stress, also no onset. This is why mental health is biopsychosocial — biology sets the stage, environment writes the script. Most modern clinicians think this way.

Recap. Six things. One — abnormal is judged by the four D's: deviance, distress, dysfunction, danger. None alone is enough. Two — the DSM-5 is the diagnostic standard in the US. Descriptive, not explanatory. Three — major categories include anxiety, OCD, trauma, mood, schizophrenia, personality, and dissociative disorders. Four — schizophrenia has positive and negative symptoms, with the dopamine hypothesis as the leading biological account. Five — personality disorders fall into three clusters: A odd, B dramatic, C anxious. Six — the diathesis-stress model: predisposition plus stress equals onset. About one in five adults will meet criteria for a disorder this year. These are common and treatable. Use respectful language. The disorder is never the person.
