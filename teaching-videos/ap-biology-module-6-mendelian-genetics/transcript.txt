Welcome back to AP Biology — Module Six, Mendelian Genetics. This kicks off College Board Unit Five, Heredity, which lands on roughly eight to eleven percent of the AP exam. We'll cover Mendel's three laws, Punnett squares, the patterns that go beyond Mendel, meiosis, and chromosomal disorders.

Two brown-eyed parents have a blue-eyed child. Not the mailman. Both parents are heterozygous — capital B, lowercase b — each carrying one hidden recessive allele. About a quarter of their children will get b from both sides and have blue eyes. One man with pea plants worked all of this out in 1865, a full century before we knew what DNA even was. His name was Gregor Mendel.

Four things to lock in today. First — Mendel's three laws. Second — Punnett squares, both the simple three-to-one cross and the four-by-four nine-three-three-one. Third — when Mendel's basic rules don't quite fit, the non-Mendelian patterns AP loves to test. And fourth — meiosis itself, linkage, and what happens when chromosomes mis-separate.

Mendel's three laws. Segregation — each parent has two alleles per gene, but gametes only carry one. The alleles split apart during meiosis one. Independent assortment — alleles for different genes sort independently of each other, as long as those genes are on different chromosomes. Dominance — in a heterozygote, only the dominant allele shows up in the phenotype; the recessive is masked. These three are the foundation; everything else in this module is either a confirmation or an exception.

Monohybrid cross — one trait, two heterozygous parents, capital A lowercase a crossed with capital A lowercase a. Draw a two-by-two grid. Mother contributes A or a along the top; father contributes A or a along the side. Fill the four cells: one AA, two Aa, one aa. That's a one-to-two-to-one genotype ratio, which collapses to a three-to-one phenotype ratio — three dominant to one recessive. Memorize three-to-one. It's the signature of a heterozygous cross.

Dihybrid cross — two traits, both heterozygous. AaBb crossed with AaBb. Each parent makes four kinds of gametes: AB, Ab, aB, ab. So you get a four-by-four Punnett with sixteen cells. Count the phenotypes: nine show both dominants, three show A dominant and b recessive, three show a recessive and B dominant, and one shows both recessives. Nine to three to three to one. The shortcut — each gene by itself still gives you a three-to-one, and you just multiply them. But this only works if the two genes are unlinked.

Six non-Mendelian patterns. Incomplete dominance — heterozygote is a blend, like pink snapdragons from red and white parents. Codominance — both alleles fully show, like AB blood type expressing both A and B antigens at the same time. Multiple alleles — more than two versions exist in the population, like the I-A, I-B, and i alleles for blood type. Pleiotropy — one gene affects many traits; the sickle-cell allele alone causes anemia, organ damage, and malaria resistance. Polygenic — many genes contribute to one trait, like height or skin color, producing a smooth distribution. And epistasis — one gene's expression depends on another, like Labrador coat color.

Sex-linked inheritance lives on the X chromosome. Females have two X's, so they need two recessive alleles to show an X-linked recessive trait. But males only have one X and a Y — we call them hemizygous — so one recessive allele is enough. That's why X-linked recessive disorders affect males far more often than females. The classic examples — red-green color blindness, hemophilia, and Duchenne muscular dystrophy. A carrier mother crossed with a normal father gives half her sons the affected condition and half her daughters as carriers.

Meiosis. One diploid germ cell becomes four haploid gametes through two divisions. Meiosis one is reductional — homologous chromosomes pair up in synapsis, cross over to exchange DNA at chiasmata, then the whole homologs separate. Diploid becomes haploid here. Meiosis two is equational — it's basically mitosis on haploid cells, where sister chromatids finally separate. Three sources of variation: crossing over in prophase one, independent assortment of homologs at metaphase one, and random fertilization. In humans, just independent assortment alone gives over eight million combinations.

Try this. A man with hemophilia, which is X-linked recessive, marries a woman who is not a carrier — completely normal. What are the genotypes and phenotypes of their daughters and their sons? Pause now. Work the Punnett. Press play when you're ready.

Answer. The father is X-lowercase-h Y — affected, hemizygous. The mother is X-capital-H X-capital-H — not a carrier. All daughters get X-capital-H from mom and X-lowercase-h from dad — so every daughter is a heterozygous carrier, but none have the disease. All sons get X-capital-H from mom and Y from dad — so every son is completely normal. The key trap: affected father plus non-carrier mother gives zero affected children, but every daughter becomes a carrier. The disease shows up again in the grandsons.

Linkage. When two genes sit close together on the same chromosome, they tend to be inherited together — they violate Mendel's law of independent assortment. We measure how often they get separated by crossing over with recombination frequency. One percent recombinant offspring equals one centimorgan of map distance. Zero percent means fully linked; fifty percent means effectively unlinked. The maximum is fifty percent because crossing over only swaps half the gametes on average. Alfred Sturtevant used this idea to build the first gene map in 1913, while he was still a sophomore in college.

When chromosomes fail to separate properly in meiosis, we call it nondisjunction. One gamete ends up with an extra chromosome; the other is missing one. Fertilize either of those and you get aneuploidy — the wrong chromosome count. Trisomy twenty-one causes Down syndrome — the most common viable autosomal trisomy. XXY is Klinefelter syndrome — males with an extra X. XO is Turner syndrome — females with only one X. Aneuploidy of full sets is called polyploidy. In plants it's common and often beneficial — wheat and strawberries are polyploid — but in animals it's almost always lethal.

Common trap — students mix up incomplete dominance and codominance. In incomplete dominance the heterozygote is a blend, an in-between, like red plus white snapdragons giving pink — one brand new intermediate phenotype, with a one-to-two-to-one ratio. In codominance both alleles fully show up at once with no blending — like AB blood type, where both A and B antigens are visible simultaneously. The trick — if the heterozygote looks new, it's incomplete. If the heterozygote shows both parent traits at once, it's codominance.

Recap. Mendel's three laws are segregation, independent assortment, and dominance. Aa by Aa gives three-to-one phenotype. AaBb by AaBb gives nine-to-three-to-three-to-one, but only if the genes are unlinked. Non-Mendelian patterns — incomplete dominance, codominance, multiple alleles, pleiotropy, polygenic, epistasis. X-linked recessive traits hit males harder because they're hemizygous. Meiosis one separates homologs, meiosis two separates sister chromatids. Linkage is measured in centimorgans. Nondisjunction causes Trisomy twenty-one, Klinefelter, and Turner.
