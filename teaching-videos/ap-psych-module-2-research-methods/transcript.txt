Welcome back. AP Psychology, Module Two — research methods. This is where psychology stops being opinions and starts being a science. By the end of today you'll know how psychologists actually figure stuff out, why most of what you see online is technically wrong, and the one thing only an experiment can prove. Let's get into it.

Quick scenario. You're scrolling TikTok and someone posts: 'People who drink coffee live longer — drink up.' They cite a real study. Sounds great, right? Honestly — no. That study almost certainly looked at coffee drinkers and non-coffee drinkers and noticed coffee drinkers lived longer. That's a correlation. It does NOT mean coffee made them live longer. Maybe coffee drinkers also tend to have steady jobs, or social lives, or money for doctors. The whole module today is about the difference between 'these two things go together' and 'this thing CAUSED that thing' — and why most headlines mix them up.

Three things today. One — the research methods hierarchy, from case study all the way up to experiment, and what each one can and cannot tell you. Two — independent variable, dependent variable, confounds. The vocabulary AP grades you on. Three — correlation versus causation, and the one number that summarizes a relationship between two variables. Buckle in.

Picture a ladder. At the bottom — case study. One person, deep dive. Great for rare conditions, terrible for generalizing. Next rung up — naturalistic observation. Watch behavior in the wild without interfering. Honest data, no control. Then survey. Ask a lot of people questions. Cheap, fast, but people lie. Above that — correlational research. Measure two things, see if they move together. Finally, top of the ladder — experiment. Manipulate one variable, randomly assign people, measure what happens. Only the top rung — the experiment — can prove causation. Memorize that ladder. AP loves it.

If you only memorize two terms from this whole module, make it these. Independent variable — the thing the experimenter changes on purpose. Dependent variable — the thing the experimenter measures to see if it changed. The dependent variable depends on the independent one. Example. You want to test if caffeine affects reaction time. You give one group caffeine, one group a placebo — that's your independent variable. Then you measure reaction time on a video game — that's your dependent variable. IV is the cause you're testing. DV is the effect you're measuring. Tattoo it on your brain.

A confound is a third variable that sneaks in and messes up your conclusion. Imagine the caffeine group also happens to be all morning people, and the placebo group is all night owls. If the caffeine group has faster reaction times, you don't know if it's the caffeine or the time-of-day preference. Time-of-day is the confound. The whole reason we do random assignment — randomly putting people into the caffeine group or the placebo group — is to spread confounds evenly so they cancel out. Random assignment is the secret sauce of a real experiment.

Two terms that sound the same and aren't. Random sampling — how you pick who's in your study. You want a sample that represents the whole population, so you pick randomly from it. Random assignment — once you have your sample, how you split them into groups. You flip a coin, basically. Random sampling helps you generalize results to the wider world. Random assignment helps you trust the cause-and-effect inside the study. Different jobs. AP will absolutely try to trick you on this. Don't fall for it.

Your turn. A psychologist wants to test whether listening to music while studying improves test scores. She finds one hundred students, randomly assigns half to study with music and half in silence, then gives them all the same quiz. What is the independent variable? What is the dependent variable? And is this an experiment or a correlation? Pause. Decide. Press play.

Welcome back. Independent variable — music versus silence. That's what she controlled. Dependent variable — quiz score. That's what she measured. And because she randomly assigned students to groups and manipulated one variable, this is an experiment. So if the music group does better, she can say music caused the higher scores — at least in her sample. If she had just asked students 'do you study with music?' and compared their grades, that would be correlation, and she could not claim music caused anything. Random assignment is what unlocks the word 'caused.'

Correlation is just a fancy word for 'these two things tend to move together.' We summarize that relationship with one number — the correlation coefficient, written as r. It runs from negative one to positive one. Positive r means as one goes up, the other goes up. Hours studied and test score — positive correlation. Negative r means as one goes up, the other goes down. Hours on TikTok and test score — negative correlation, sorry. The sign tells you the direction. The size — how close r is to one or negative one — tells you the strength. An r of zero point nine is strong. An r of zero point one is basically nothing. Sign and strength. Two different things.

Real-world example you have to know. Ice cream sales and drowning deaths are positively correlated. When ice cream sales go up, drownings go up. Did ice cream cause drowning? No. Both go up in summer because it's hot. Hot weather is the actual cause — the third variable. This is the famous case AP uses to drill in 'correlation does not equal causation.' Whenever you see a study that says 'X is linked to Y' or 'X is associated with Y' — those are correlation words. They're telling you the study cannot prove cause. It can only prove the two things move together.

Three more terms and we're done. The experimental group gets the treatment — the actual caffeine, the actual drug, the actual music. The control group gets nothing, or a placebo — a fake pill that looks identical. Why? Because just thinking you got a drug can make you feel better. That's the placebo effect, and it's real. The best studies are double-blind — neither the participants nor the researcher know who got the real treatment until the data is in. That stops bias on both sides. And finally — statistical significance, usually written p less than point zero five. At AP level, just know this means: the result was unlikely to happen by random chance. It probably reflects a real effect. We're not memorizing the formula. Just the intuition.

Recap. The hierarchy — case study, naturalistic observation, survey, correlation, experiment. Only experiments prove causation. Independent variable is what you change. Dependent variable is what you measure. A confound is a sneaky third variable, and random assignment is how we kill confounds. Random sampling and random assignment are different jobs. Correlation coefficient r runs from negative one to positive one — sign for direction, size for strength. Correlation does not equal causation — see ice cream and drowning. Double-blind plus a placebo plus a p value below point zero five is what a serious experiment looks like.
