Here's the headline. Therapy isn't soft pseudoscience anymore. Cognitive behavioral therapy for depression has more empirical support than most prescription drugs. SSRIs help millions of people every day. Modern electroconvulsive therapy — yes, the one with the scary reputation — is one of the most effective treatments we have for severe depression that won't budge any other way. Module Thirteen we learned what can go wrong. Today — one hundred years of figuring out what helps. None of it is magic. All of it is evidence.

Three things today. One — the five major psychological therapies. Psychodynamic, behavioral, cognitive, cognitive-behavioral, and humanistic. Two — biomedical treatments. Drug therapy, electroconvulsive therapy, and a brief mention of newer brain stimulation methods. Three — does any of this actually work? The short answer is yes, and we'll look at the evidence. By the end you'll be able to read a description of a treatment session and name the approach.

Approach one — psychoanalytic and psychodynamic therapy. This is the Freud heritage. The core idea — your current symptoms are the surface of unconscious conflicts, often rooted in childhood. The job of therapy is to make the unconscious conscious. Three classic techniques. Free association — the person says whatever comes to mind, no filter, and the therapist listens for patterns. Dream analysis — dreams are treated as a window into hidden conflicts. Transference — the person starts unconsciously treating the therapist like an important figure from their past, like a parent, and that becomes the material to work through. Modern psychodynamic therapy is shorter, more focused, and less about Freud's specific theories — but the central move is the same. Use the relationship in the room to get at what the person can't see in themselves.

Approach two — behavioral therapy. The premise is the opposite of psychodynamic. Forget the unconscious. The behavior IS the problem, and the behavior was learned, which means it can be unlearned. Two big toolkits here. Classical conditioning gives us systematic desensitization for phobias — pair the feared thing with deep relaxation, gradually, up a fear hierarchy, until the fear extinguishes. Exposure therapy is the broader family — gradual or full exposure to the feared situation without escape, so the brain learns it's actually safe. Aversive conditioning pairs an unwanted behavior with something unpleasant — used carefully, in narrow cases. Operant conditioning gives us token economies, common in classrooms and inpatient units — earn tokens for desired behavior, exchange for privileges. Behavior modification more broadly. Behavioral therapy works fastest and most reliably for anxiety, phobias, and OCD.

Approach three — cognitive therapy. Aaron Beck and Albert Ellis. The premise — it's not the event that upsets you, it's how you interpret the event. A friend doesn't text back. One person thinks 'they're busy.' Another thinks 'they hate me, no one likes me, I'm worthless.' Same event. Two very different feelings. Cognitive therapy targets the distorted thoughts. Beck's most famous contribution is the cognitive triad of depression. Negative views of the self — I'm worthless. Negative views of the world — everything is unfair. Negative views of the future — nothing will ever get better. The therapy work is to identify those automatic thoughts, examine the evidence, and replace them with more accurate, more flexible ones.

Approach four — cognitive-behavioral therapy. CBT. This is the integration of approaches two and three, and it is the most empirically-supported psychotherapy we have. CBT is the front-line treatment for depression, anxiety disorders, OCD, and PTSD. It's structured. It's short — typically twelve to twenty sessions. And it's homework-heavy. Three classic CBT tools. Thought records — write down the situation, the automatic thought, the emotion, and then challenge the thought with evidence. Behavioral experiments — instead of arguing about whether the feared thing will happen, go test it. Behavioral activation — when depressed, the person stops doing things, which makes the depression worse, so we schedule meaningful activities back into the week. CBT works because it changes thinking AND changes behavior, at the same time, in measurable ways. If you remember one therapy from this module, remember CBT.

Welcome back. The approach is cognitive-behavioral therapy. The specific tool is a thought record. Notice how it does both jobs at once. The cognitive piece — identifying and challenging the automatic thought. The behavioral piece — building the active habit of doing it on paper, every day. That double-action is exactly why CBT outperforms either approach alone for depression and anxiety.

Approach five — humanistic therapy. Carl Rogers and his client-centered therapy. Maslow is in this family too. The premise here is the most optimistic of the bunch — people have an innate drive to grow, to self-actualize, and the therapist's job is to create the conditions where that drive can do its work. Three Rogerian conditions. Unconditional positive regard — the person is accepted as they are, not graded. Genuineness — the therapist is real, not playing a role. Empathy — actively trying to feel what the person is feeling. The active technique is reflective listening — the therapist mirrors back what the person said, which sounds simple but is enormously powerful. The person hears their own words, often for the first time, and starts working things out. Humanistic therapy is the one that most influenced how all therapists, of every approach, talk to people.

Now the biomedical side. When the brain is the problem, you change the brain. Drug therapy comes in four big categories the AP exam wants you to know. Antidepressants — most commonly SSRIs like fluoxetine, which raise serotonin levels. Used for depression, anxiety, OCD. Antipsychotics, used for schizophrenia, mostly work by blocking dopamine receptors. Anxiolytics — anti-anxiety drugs — most often benzodiazepines for short-term use. Mood stabilizers — lithium, the standard for bipolar disorder, smooths out the highs and lows. Beyond drugs. Electroconvulsive therapy — ECT — is used for severe depression that hasn't responded to anything else. It's done under general anesthesia, with muscle relaxants, and it works. The pop-culture version is decades out of date. Modern ECT is safe, controlled, and often the most effective option for treatment-resistant depression. Briefly — TMS, transcranial magnetic stimulation, uses magnetic pulses to stimulate brain regions, no anesthesia needed. Psychosurgery, like the lobotomies of the nineteen forties, is essentially never done today.

Does any of this actually work. Yes. Meta-analyses, which combine the results of hundreds of studies, consistently show that people who get therapy improve at much higher rates than people who don't. Roughly seventy-five percent of people in therapy show meaningful improvement, compared to about thirty-five percent who improve without treatment. CBT has the strongest evidence for depression and anxiety. For moderate to severe cases, the combination of therapy plus medication usually outperforms either one alone. And here's the part that surprises people. A lot of what makes therapy work cuts across approaches. Therapeutic alliance — the trust between the person and the therapist — predicts outcomes more than the specific technique. Hope. Working through difficult material in a safe place. Reversing learned helplessness. These common factors matter, no matter which lens the therapist uses.

Recap. Six things. One — psychoanalytic and psychodynamic therapy work with the unconscious — free association, dream analysis, transference. Two — behavioral therapy retrains learned responses — systematic desensitization, exposure, token economies. Three — cognitive therapy targets distorted thoughts. Beck's cognitive triad — negative self, world, future. Four — cognitive-behavioral therapy combines them and is the front-line treatment for depression, anxiety, OCD, and PTSD. Five — humanistic therapy uses unconditional positive regard, genuineness, empathy, and reflective listening. Six — biomedical treatments include SSRIs, antipsychotics, anxiolytics, lithium, and modern ECT for treatment-resistant depression. Therapy works — about seventy-five percent improve. CBT plus meds is often the strongest combination for moderate-to-severe cases. The therapeutic alliance is the common factor across every approach.
