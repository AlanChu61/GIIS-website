Welcome to AP Biology, Module One — Chemistry of Life. This is Unit One of the College Board curriculum, and it shows up on roughly eight to eleven percent of the AP exam. Everything you learn this year — cells, genetics, evolution — sits on top of what we cover today. Let's build the foundation.

Here is a puzzle. Ice floats on water, even though almost every other solid sinks in its own liquid. And water can climb a hundred-foot tree against gravity. Both of these strange behaviors trace back to one tiny feature of the water molecule — and that same feature is also the reason you exist. Let's find out what it is.

Here is the game plan. First — water and carbon, the two chemistry stories that make life possible. Second — the four biological macromolecules: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids. Third — the two reactions that build them up and break them down. Three pieces, and you have all the chemistry you need for the rest of AP Biology.

About twenty-five elements are essential to life, but just four of them — carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen — make up ninety-six percent of living matter by mass. Add phosphorus and sulfur and you get the acronym CHNOPS. Inside every atom, protons and neutrons sit in the nucleus, and electrons occupy orbitals within energy levels — or shells — around it. That electron arrangement is what decides how atoms bond.

Three bond types you must know. Covalent bonds share electrons and are strong — think carbon-carbon, carbon-hydrogen, and peptide bonds. Ionic bonds transfer electrons; opposite charges attract, but they weaken in water. A hydrogen bond is more specific than just any attraction — it requires a donor, which is a hydrogen covalently bonded to nitrogen, oxygen, or fluorine, and an acceptor, which is another N, O, or F atom with a lone pair. Each one is weak — about five percent of a covalent bond — but collectively they're powerful.

Water is polar because oxygen pulls electrons harder than hydrogen, and the molecule bends at about a hundred and four point five degrees. The oxygen end is slightly negative; each hydrogen is slightly positive. That polarity creates hydrogen bonding between water molecules, and from that one feature you get six emergent properties: cohesion, adhesion, high specific heat, high heat of vaporization, ice less dense than liquid water, and water as a versatile solvent. Quick AP note — water dissolves polar and ionic substances, but it does not dissolve nonpolar ones. Those get pushed together by the hydrophobic effect — and that is how we will explain lipid bilayers later.

Let's tie those properties to real biology. Trees pull water up the xylem using cohesion — water sticking to water — and adhesion — water sticking to the vessel walls. You cool down by sweating because water's high heat of vaporization carries heat away. Lakes freeze from the top because ice is less dense, and that ice insulates the fish below. Salt dissolves because water molecules form hydration shells around the ions. And your body temperature stays stable thanks to water's high specific heat.

Carbon is the backbone of life because it has four valence electrons and forms four covalent bonds. It makes single and double bonds throughout biology — triple bonds are very rare in living systems. Carbon builds straight chains, branched chains, and rings, and the same formula with different structures gives you isomers. Now meet the functional groups — hydroxyl is polar and forms hydrogen bonds, carboxyl is acidic and negatively charged at physiological pH, amino is basic and positively charged, sulfhydryl pairs up into disulfide bridges, phosphate carries a strong negative charge on every nucleotide and every ATP, and methyl is nonpolar and marks DNA and proteins.

Here is the build-and-break rule for all the macromolecules. Dehydration synthesis joins two monomers and releases a water molecule — you remove water to build the bond. Hydrolysis is the reverse — you add water to break the bond. One important asterisk: three of the four macromolecules are true polymers, but lipids are not. Even so, lipids are still built by dehydration synthesis — each fatty acid loses a water when it attaches to glycerol. So whether something is a polymer, and whether it is built by dehydration synthesis, are two different questions.

Try this — two prompts. First: a protein is being broken down into its individual amino acids during digestion. Which reaction is happening — dehydration synthesis or hydrolysis? And does it consume water, or release water? Second prompt — the reverse direction: at the ribosome, your cell is building a brand new protein by linking amino acids together. Which reaction is that, and does it consume or release water? Pause now. Solve it. Press play when you're ready.

Answers. The first one — protein digestion — is hydrolysis. Breaking the polymer into monomers requires adding a water molecule across each peptide bond, so hydrolysis consumes water. The second one — building a new protein at the ribosome — is dehydration synthesis. Every new peptide bond formed releases one water molecule. Remember the trick: dehydration removes water to build; hydrolysis adds water to break.

Carbohydrates first. The monomer is the monosaccharide — glucose, formula C-six H-twelve O-six. Starch, glycogen, and cellulose are all made of glucose, but the bond geometry changes everything. Starch uses alpha-one-four bonds and your enzymes can digest it. Cellulose uses beta-one-four bonds — humans simply do not make that enzyme, which is why salad is fiber, not fuel. Now lipids — and these are not true polymers. Triglycerides are glycerol plus three fatty acids. Unsaturated fatty acids have carbon-carbon double bonds that put a kink in the chain — kinks prevent close packing, which means liquid at room temperature, like plant oils. Saturated fats have no kinks and stay solid. And phospholipids have a hydrophilic head plus two hydrophobic tails, which is exactly what makes them form the lipid bilayer of every cell membrane.

Proteins. The monomer is the amino acid — there are twenty standard ones. Each has a central alpha carbon bonded to an amino group, a carboxyl group, a hydrogen, and a variable R-group. Amino acids link by peptide bonds — that is dehydration synthesis. Proteins fold in four levels: primary is the linear sequence held by covalent peptide bonds, secondary is alpha helices and beta sheets held by backbone hydrogen bonds, tertiary is the overall three-D fold driven by R-group interactions, and quaternary is multiple subunits coming together — hemoglobin has four. Heat or pH can denature a protein by disrupting those non-covalent interactions — but the peptide bonds, the primary sequence, are not broken. And per OpenStax Chapter Three-Four, proteins serve as enzymes, structural support like collagen and keratin, transporters like hemoglobin, and defenders like antibodies.

Nucleic acids. The monomer is the nucleotide — a sugar, a phosphate, and a nitrogenous base. Nucleotides link together by phosphodiester bonds — that is dehydration synthesis between the three-prime hydroxyl of one sugar and the five-prime phosphate of the next. So every strand has a five-prime end and a three-prime end, and grows in a specific direction. The sugar-phosphate backbone runs along the outside; the bases point inward. DNA is double-stranded and the two strands are antiparallel — one runs five to three while its partner runs three to five. Adenine pairs with thymine via two hydrogen bonds, guanine pairs with cytosine via three — that is why GC-rich DNA is more stable. And don't forget — ATP is also a nucleotide, your cell's energy currency.

Recap. Water's polarity gives us six life-supporting properties — and remember, water dissolves polar and ionic substances but excludes nonpolar ones via the hydrophobic effect. Carbon's four bonds plus its functional groups give us molecular diversity, and each group's polarity predicts where it appears. The four macromolecules are carbs, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids — only lipids are not true polymers, but they're still built by dehydration synthesis. Proteins fold in four levels, and denaturation disrupts the last three but preserves the primary sequence. And the rule for everything: dehydration removes water to build; hydrolysis adds water to break.
