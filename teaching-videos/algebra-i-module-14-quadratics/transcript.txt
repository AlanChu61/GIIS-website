Welcome back. Algebra One, Module Fourteen — quadratic equations. The boss fight of Algebra One. By the end of today, you'll have three different ways to solve any quadratic, plus a single number that tells you how many solutions exist before you even start.

Throw a ball. The path it traces through the air? A parabola. The math behind that path? Quadratic equations. They show up everywhere — physics for projectile motion, business for profit curves, engineering for arch bridges, even in stock market modeling. Anything that goes up, peaks, and comes back down is probably governed by a quadratic. Today we learn to solve them.

Six big ideas. One — what a quadratic equation looks like. Two — Method One, factoring. Three — Method Two, completing the square. Four — Method Three, the quadratic formula. Five — the discriminant, that magic number that predicts how many solutions you'll get. Six — when to use which method, because some are easier than others depending on the problem.

Standard form. A quadratic equation looks like a x squared plus b x plus c equals zero. The x squared is the boss — that's what makes it quadratic. The a, b, and c are just numbers — coefficients. The only rule is a can't be zero, because if a is zero, the x squared disappears and you're back to a linear equation.

Method One — factoring. The idea is simple. If you can rewrite the quadratic as two factors multiplied together equaling zero, like x minus r times x minus s equals zero, then either x minus r equals zero, or x minus s equals zero. Two things multiply to zero, at least one of them has to be zero. So x equals r or x equals s. Factoring works fast when the numbers are nice.

Example. Solve x squared plus five x plus six equals zero. We want two numbers that multiply to six and add to five. Two and three. So we factor as x plus two times x plus three equals zero. Set each factor to zero. x plus two equals zero gives x equals negative two. x plus three equals zero gives x equals negative three. Two solutions. Done.

Your turn. Solve x squared minus seven x plus twelve equals zero by factoring. You need two numbers that multiply to twelve and add to negative seven. Pause and try.

Time. The numbers are negative three and negative four. They multiply to twelve, they add to negative seven. So we factor as x minus three times x minus four equals zero. x equals three or x equals four. Both work. Plug them back to check if you want — three squared minus seven times three plus twelve is nine minus twenty-one plus twelve, equals zero. Verified.

Method Two — completing the square. Used when factoring isn't obvious. The idea is to manipulate the equation into the form some-binomial squared equals a number, then take the square root of both sides. It's more steps than factoring but it always works, and it's the proof technique behind the quadratic formula.

Example. Solve x squared plus six x plus five equals zero. Move the constant to the right — x squared plus six x equals negative five. Now take half of the b coefficient, which is six, half is three, square it, get nine. Add nine to both sides. Left side becomes x plus three, all squared, equals four. Take the square root — x plus three equals plus or minus two. So x equals negative one or x equals negative five.

Method Three — the quadratic formula. The universal solver. It works for every quadratic, even the ugly ones with decimals or irrational solutions. Memorize this one if you memorize anything from Algebra One. It will pay rent for you for the next four years of math.

The formula. x equals negative b, plus or minus the square root of b squared minus four a c, all over two a. Say it out loud a few times. Negative b plus or minus square root of b squared minus four a c, over two a. Once you've said it enough times, it becomes muscle memory.

Example. Solve x squared minus four x minus five equals zero. Identify a, b, c. a is one, b is negative four, c is negative five. Plug in. x equals four, plus or minus square root of sixteen plus twenty, over two. Sixteen plus twenty is thirty-six. Square root of thirty-six is six. So x equals four plus six over two, or four minus six over two. That's five, or negative one. Two solutions. Same answers factoring would give — but the formula didn't need us to spot the factors.

Round two. Use the quadratic formula on two x squared plus three x minus five equals zero. a is two, b is three, c is negative five. Pause and solve.

Welcome back. b squared minus four a c is nine plus forty, equals forty-nine. Square root is seven. So x equals negative three plus seven over four, or negative three minus seven over four. That's one, or negative five halves. Both check out. If you got it — you've earned the right to call yourself fluent in quadratics.

Now the bonus tool. The discriminant is the part under the square root in the quadratic formula — b squared minus four a c. By itself, it tells you how many real solutions a quadratic has, before you do any of the actual solving. It's a peek at the answer.

Three cases. If the discriminant is positive, you get two real solutions — the parabola crosses the x-axis twice. If it equals zero, you get one solution, sometimes called a repeated root — the parabola just touches the x-axis at its vertex. If it's negative, zero real solutions — the parabola never crosses the x-axis. The solutions are imaginary, which you'll handle in Algebra Two.

When to use which method. Try factoring first if the coefficients are small whole numbers — it's fastest when it works. Use completing the square when a is one and b is even — clean arithmetic. Use the quadratic formula whenever factoring fails or the numbers are ugly — it always works. The discriminant first if you only need to know HOW MANY solutions, not what they are.

Common mistakes to dodge. One — sign errors when identifying b and c, especially with negatives. Write a, b, c on the page first, with their signs. Two — forgetting BOTH plus and minus solutions. Quadratics give two answers, not one. Three — misreading the formula as negative b over two a plus the square root, instead of negative b plus or minus square root, all divided by two a. The whole top is divided by two a — not just the negative b part.

Recap. Quadratic equations are a x squared plus b x plus c equals zero. Three methods to solve them — factoring when easy, completing the square when symmetric, quadratic formula always. The discriminant — b squared minus four a c — tells you how many real solutions before you start. Two if positive, one if zero, zero if negative.
