AP Calculus AB. Module Four — contextual applications of differentiation. This is where the derivative stops being abstract and starts describing the real world. Motion, related rates, linear approximation, and the rule that rescues impossible-looking limits. Roughly nine minutes — let's go.

A ten-foot ladder is leaning against a wall. Someone kicks the base, and it starts sliding away at a steady one foot per second. Common-sense question — does the top of the ladder slide down the wall at one foot per second too? Surprisingly, no. As the ladder flattens, the top speeds up — and the derivative is going to tell us exactly how fast.

Three big ideas today. One — the derivative as a real-world rate, with units. Two — related rates, when two quantities change together in time. Three — linearization for approximation, and L'Hospital's Rule for indeterminate limits. By the end you'll handle every flavor of applied-derivative problem the AP exam throws at you.

Rule number one of applied calculus — always write the units. If f measures something and x measures something else, then f-prime is a ratio: output units per input unit. If C of x is the cost in dollars to produce x widgets, then C-prime of x is in dollars per widget. Forgetting units is the single easiest way to lose points on the AP free response.

Motion is the cleanest application. Position s of t. Velocity v of t is the derivative of position. Acceleration a of t is the derivative of velocity, or the second derivative of position. Speed is the absolute value of velocity — and here's the rule students mix up: speed is increasing when velocity and acceleration have the same sign. Same sign, speeding up. Opposite signs, slowing down.

Let's try one. Position is t cubed minus six t squared plus nine t. Velocity is three t squared minus twelve t plus nine, which factors as three times t minus one, times t minus three. At t equals four, velocity is positive nine, and acceleration — six t minus twelve — is positive twelve. Same sign, so the particle is speeding up at t equals four.

Same idea, different units. Water flowing into a tank — d V d t in liters per minute. A population growing — d P d t in people per year. Marginal cost — d C d x in dollars per item. The math is identical. Only the units change. Every applied derivative is a ratio of how-much-of-this per how-much-of-that.

Related rates link two changing quantities through one equation. Here's the four-step method — memorize this. Step one: draw and label the picture. Step two: name your variables as functions of time. Step three: write an equation relating them. Step four: differentiate with respect to t — and only THEN plug in numbers. Substituting too early is the number one mistake on this topic.

Back to the ladder. The wall, the floor, and the ladder form a right triangle, so x squared plus y squared equals one hundred. Differentiate with respect to t: two x times d x d t plus two y times d y d t equals zero. When y is six, x is eight, and d x d t is one. Solve: d y d t equals negative eight-thirds — about minus one-point-three-three feet per second. The negative sign tells us the top is falling, and it's falling faster than the base is sliding.

Your turn. A spherical balloon is being inflated. Its radius grows at two centimeters per second. How fast is the volume changing when the radius reaches five centimeters? Remember — V equals four-thirds pi r cubed. Pause now. Solve it. Press play when you're ready.

Walking through it. Differentiate V with respect to t: d V d t equals four pi r squared times d r d t. Substitute r equals five and d r d t equals two. Four pi times twenty-five times two equals two hundred pi cubic centimeters per second — roughly six hundred twenty-eight. Notice the units — cubic centimeters per second — they come straight from the formula, not from us guessing.

Near a point, a smooth curve looks just like its tangent line. That tangent line has a name — the linearization. L of x equals f of a plus f-prime of a times x minus a. When do you reach for it? When you need to approximate f at some value close to a point where f and f-prime are easy to compute exactly. To estimate the square root of four-point-one, anchor at a equals four, because we know the square root of four is exactly two. f-prime is one over two root x, which at four is one-fourth. So L of four-point-one is two plus one-fourth times zero-point-one — that's two-point-zero-two-five. The true value is two-point-zero-two-four-eight. Stunningly close.

L'Hospital's Rule rescues limits that look like zero over zero or infinity over infinity. The rule: take the derivative of the top and the derivative of the bottom — separately, not the quotient rule — and try the limit again. Two refinements you need to know. First — other indeterminate forms like zero times infinity, infinity minus infinity, or one to the infinity must be rearranged algebraically into zero-over-zero or infinity-over-infinity FIRST. For instance, x times natural log of x as x approaches zero from the right is zero times infinity. Rewrite it as ln x over one-over-x — now it's infinity over infinity, and L'Hospital applies. Second — you can apply L'Hospital REPEATEDLY. The limit of one minus cosine x over x squared is zero over zero. One application gives sine x over two x, still zero over zero. A second application gives cosine x over two, which is one-half.

Here is the classic AP trap. L'Hospital's Rule only works on indeterminate forms. Sine x over x at x equals zero is zero over zero — legal. But if someone tries to apply L'Hospital to cosine x over one at x equals zero, that's already one over one. It is not indeterminate. Applying L'Hospital is illegal here, even if you accidentally get the right answer. Always check the form FIRST. Zero over zero, or infinity over infinity — those are the only two doorways in.

Recap. Units of f-prime are units of f over units of x — always write them. Position, velocity, acceleration form a derivative chain, and speed increases only when v and a share a sign. Related rates: differentiate with respect to t before substituting. Linearization is the tangent line, used to approximate near a friendly anchor point. L'Hospital's Rule applies only to zero over zero or infinity over infinity — check the form first.
