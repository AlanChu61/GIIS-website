AP Calculus AB. Module Seven — Riemann sums and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. This is Unit Six, the highest-weighted unit on the AP exam. Today we tie the two halves of calculus together — derivatives and integrals — and we name the theorem that connects them.

Push your foot on the gas. Your velocity-versus-time graph climbs. Now look at the shaded area under that curve from time zero to time two seconds. That area is how far you actually traveled. Velocity is a rate. Area under a rate is total amount. That's integration in one picture — and that's what we're going to make precise.

Three moves today. One — Riemann sums: chop the area into rectangles to approximate it. Two — take the limit, and the sum becomes the definite integral. Three — the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, both parts, which connects integrals back to derivatives. Two parts to that theorem. You need both.

Big picture first. If f of t is a RATE — speed, flow rate, rate of population growth — then the integral from a to b of f of t d t is the total accumulated amount over that interval. Rate times time. That's all integration is doing. The integral sign is just a fancy way of saying: add up rate times time over the whole interval.

Here's the mechanic. Take the interval from a to b. Slice it into n equal sub-intervals each of width delta x equals b minus a over n. Build rectangles on each one. You get four standard choices — left endpoint, right endpoint, midpoint, or trapezoidal. Each one approximates the area under f. Narrower slices, better estimate.

Free AP exam point. On an INCREASING function, left-endpoint sums UNDER-estimate and right-endpoint sums OVER-estimate. On a DECREASING function, it flips — left over-estimates, right under-estimates. Midpoint usually beats both. Memorize this. They ask it on the multiple choice every single year.

Now the leap. Take n to infinity. The slices become infinitely thin. The sum becomes the integral. Sigma turns into the elongated S. Delta x becomes d x. The integral from a to b of f of x d x — by definition — is the limit of those Riemann sums. And the sample point inside each sub-interval can be left, right, midpoint, anywhere — they all converge to the same number.

Listen carefully — this trips up half the AP class. The definite integral is a NUMBER. Not a function. Once you plug in the upper and lower bounds, there's no x left in the answer. If your answer still has x in it, you forgot to evaluate. The indefinite integral — that's a family of functions, plus C. Different beast. Coming in Module Eight.

Five properties to memorize. One — the integral from a to a is zero. Same point to itself, no area. Two — flipping the bounds flips the sign. The integral from b to a equals negative the integral from a to b. Circle that one — it's the single most-missed property on the AP. Three — additivity: you can split at any interior point c. Four — constants pull out. Five — sums split into the sum of the integrals.

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part Two — the evaluation theorem. If big F is an antiderivative of little f, then the integral from a to b of f of x d x equals F of b minus F of a. Watch the example. Integral from one to three of two x d x. Antiderivative of two x is x squared. Plug in three, get nine. Plug in one, get one. Subtract — eight. That's the integral.

Your turn. Evaluate the integral from zero to two of three x squared plus one, d x, using FTC Part Two. Find the antiderivative. Plug in two. Plug in zero. Subtract. Pause the video. Solve it. Press play when you're ready.

Walking it through. Antiderivative of three x squared is x cubed — power rule in reverse. Antiderivative of one is x. So big F of x is x cubed plus x. F of two is eight plus two, which is ten. F of zero is zero. Ten minus zero equals ten. The integral equals ten.

FTC Part One — the accumulation theorem. Define big F of x as the integral from a to x of f of t d t. Then F prime of x equals f of x. Differentiation and integration undo each other. Two critical warnings. The variable inside the integral is t, a dummy variable — it does not appear in the answer. And if the upper bound is a function g of x instead of just x, chain rule kicks in — you multiply by g prime of x.

This is the most-misstated theorem on the AP exam. The derivative of the integral from a to x of f of t d t is f of x — the upper-bound variable goes inside f. NOT f of t. NOT capital F of x. NOT zero. The t inside is a dummy variable, it gets eaten. The answer is f evaluated at the upper bound. Memorize the right column.

Recap. Integration is accumulation — signed area under a rate. Riemann sums, left right midpoint trapezoid, approximate; take the limit, you get the definite integral. The definite integral is a number, not a function. Five properties including the bound-swap sign flip. FTC Part One — derivative of an accumulation function is f at the upper bound. FTC Part Two — F of b minus F of a evaluates definite integrals. Two parts, one theorem, the whole engine of calculus.
