Psychology started as philosophy. For centuries, people who wondered about the mind were just called philosophers. The shift happened in eighteen seventy-nine, when Wilhelm Wundt opened the first psychology lab in Leipzig, Germany. He decided the mind could be studied with experiments — measured, timed, recorded — not just argued about. That's the moment psychology became a science. Wundt's date — eighteen seventy-nine. Memorize it. AP loves that date.

Two early schools came right after Wundt. Structuralism, led by Edward Titchener, tried to break consciousness down into its smallest parts using introspection — basically asking people to report what was in their head. Functionalism, led by William James, asked instead — what is the mind FOR? Why did it evolve? Structuralism died out. Functionalism is basically the seed of modern cognitive science.

Now the six modern approaches. You will see all six on the AP exam. Memorize them. One — biological. The mind is the brain. Look at neurons, hormones, genes. Two — cognitive. The mind is information processing. How do we encode, store, retrieve. Three — behavioral. Forget the inside — watch what people do. Stimuli and responses. Four — psychodynamic. Freud's descendants. Unconscious motives, childhood, conflict. Five — humanistic. People want to grow and self-actualize. Maslow, Rogers. Six — sociocultural. Behavior depends on culture and context. Same person acts differently in different rooms.

Quick test. Someone is afraid of dogs. What would each approach say? Biological — maybe an overactive amygdala or anxious genetics. Cognitive — she's catastrophizing, expecting attack. Behavioral — she was bitten once, classical conditioning did the rest. Psychodynamic — the dog represents some unconscious threat from childhood. Humanistic — fear is blocking her from being her full self. Sociocultural — she grew up in a culture where dogs are unclean. Same fear. Six explanations. None complete on their own.

Your turn. A researcher gives one group of students a lavender room and another group a sterile white room and measures their stress levels with a saliva test. Which approach is this researcher using? Pause the video. Decide. Press play.

Welcome back. The hard part: there are two right answers depending on what they measure. The saliva test, looking at cortisol, is biological. But the room — environment shaping behavior — that's sociocultural. The combination is what we now call biopsychosocial — looking at biology, psychology, and social context all at once. Most modern researchers do this. They don't pick one lens. They use whichever lens answers the question.

One more idea. Two huge waves shaped modern psychology. The cognitive revolution in the nineteen fifties brought back the inside of the mind after decades of strict behaviorism. And positive psychology, led by Martin Seligman in the late nineteen nineties, asked — instead of just studying disorders, what makes people flourish? Both shifts are still shaping research today.

Where does this matter? Every time you read a headline like 'study finds depression is genetic' or 'study finds depression is caused by social media' — that's not contradiction. That's two approaches answering different questions. The honest answer is usually both. AP wants you to recognize that, and to identify which approach a study is using.

Recap. Psychology was philosophy until eighteen seventy-nine, when Wundt opened the first lab. Structuralism and functionalism were the early schools. Modern psychology has six approaches — biological, cognitive, behavioral, psychodynamic, humanistic, sociocultural. None owns the truth. The biopsychosocial model combines them. Two big waves shaped today's field — the cognitive revolution and positive psychology.
