## Elements of Life

- ~25 elements are essential to living organisms; **four make up 96% of living
  matter**: **carbon (C), hydrogen (H), oxygen (O), nitrogen (N)**.
- Often abbreviated as **CHNOPS** when including **phosphorus (P)** and
  **sulfur (S)**, which are also essential.
- Other trace elements include calcium, potassium, sodium, magnesium, iron, etc.
- **Atomic structure**: atoms consist of protons (+) and neutrons (neutral) in
  the nucleus, with electrons (−) in shells around the nucleus.

## Chemical Bonds

| Bond type | Formed by | Strength (relative) | Where seen |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Covalent bond** | Sharing electrons | Strong | C–C, C–H, peptide bonds |
| **Ionic bond** | Transfer of electrons; opposite charges attract | Medium (weak in water) | NaCl, salt bridges in proteins |
| **Hydrogen bond** | Weak attraction between δ⁺ H and δ⁻ atom (usually O, N, F) | Weak individually, collectively strong | Water cohesion, DNA base pairing, protein secondary structure |

- **Polar covalent bond**: electrons shared unequally → partial charges
  (e.g., O–H in water).
- **Nonpolar covalent bond**: electrons shared equally (e.g., C–H, C–C).

## Properties of Water

Water (H₂O) is **polar** because oxygen is more electronegative than hydrogen,
giving the molecule a bent (~104.5°) shape with δ⁻ on O and δ⁺ on each H.
Hydrogen bonding between water molecules explains every emergent property below.

- **Cohesion**: water molecules stick to each other (drives transpiration in
  plants, surface tension).
- **Adhesion**: water sticks to other polar surfaces (capillary action in
  xylem).
- **High specific heat**: large heat capacity stabilizes temperature in cells
  and oceans (helps homeostasis).
- **High heat of vaporization**: evaporation removes a lot of heat — basis of
  evaporative cooling (sweating).
- **Ice less dense than liquid water**: hydrogen bonds form a crystal lattice
  with more space — ice floats, insulating water below.
- **Versatile solvent (often called "the solvent of life")**: dissolves polar
  and ionic substances via hydration shells. It does **NOT** dissolve nonpolar
  substances — those experience the **hydrophobic effect** (water is excluded,
  pushing nonpolar molecules together; foreshadows lipid bilayers).
  *Note: many texts say "universal solvent" colloquially, but on AP exams
  prefer "versatile" or "excellent solvent for polar and ionic substances."*

### Hydrogen bond mechanics

A hydrogen bond requires a **donor** (H covalently bonded to a strongly
electronegative atom: N, O, or F) and an **acceptor** (an N/O/F atom with a
lone pair). The bond itself is a weak electrostatic attraction between the
δ⁺ hydrogen and the lone pair on the acceptor — not a shared-electron
covalent bond. Individually weak (~5% of a covalent bond), but cumulatively
strong because biological molecules form many of them.

### Atomic structure note

In modern (post-Bohr) chemistry, electrons occupy **orbitals** (probability
clouds) within **energy levels / shells** — not fixed planetary "orbits."
The 96% figure for C/H/O/N is **by mass** of living matter.

## Carbon Chemistry

- Carbon has **4 valence electrons** → can form **4 covalent bonds**.
- Forms single and double bonds throughout biology; triple bonds are very
  rare in standard biological molecules.
- Can build straight chains, branched chains, and rings.
- **Isomers**: same molecular formula, different structures (structural,
  geometric/cis-trans, enantiomers).
- **Functional groups** common in biology — each has **distinct properties**
  that determine where it appears and how it reacts:

| Group | Notation | Polarity / Charge at neutral pH | Where it shows up |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hydroxyl | –OH | polar; forms H-bonds | sugars, alcohols, serine/threonine |
| Carbonyl | C=O | polar | aldehydes, ketones, peptide bonds |
| Carboxyl | –COOH | acidic; loses H⁺ → –COO⁻ at physiological pH | amino acids, fatty acids |
| Amino | –NH₂ | basic; gains H⁺ → –NH₃⁺ at physiological pH | amino acids |
| Sulfhydryl | –SH | mildly polar; pairs of –SH form disulfide bridges (–S–S–) | cysteine; tertiary protein structure |
| Phosphate | –OPO₃²⁻ (often written –PO₄) | strongly negative charge | nucleotides, ATP, phospholipids |
| Methyl | –CH₃ | nonpolar; hydrophobic | side chains; DNA/protein methylation marks |

## The Four Biological Macromolecules

Built by **dehydration synthesis** (forming bonds, releasing H₂O) and broken
by **hydrolysis** (adding H₂O to break bonds). All four are polymers (except
most lipids).

### 1. Carbohydrates
- **Monomer**: monosaccharide (e.g., glucose C₆H₁₂O₆).
- Disaccharides: sucrose (glucose + fructose), lactose, maltose.
- **Polysaccharides** — same monomer (glucose), but the bond geometry is
  decisive:
  - **Starch** — plant energy storage; α-glucose linked by **α-1,4 glycosidic
    bonds**; forms helical, easily digestible chains.
  - **Glycogen** — animal energy storage in liver/muscle; α-glucose, α-1,4
    backbone with α-1,6 branches every ~10 residues.
  - **Cellulose** — plant cell walls; β-glucose linked by **β-1,4 glycosidic
    bonds**. Alternating monomer orientation produces straight chains that
    hydrogen-bond into rigid microfibrils. Humans lack the enzyme to hydrolyze
    β-1,4 linkages → cellulose is dietary fiber, not nutrition.
  - **Chitin** — modified β-glucose monomers; fungal cell walls and arthropod
    exoskeletons.
- **Function**: energy source, energy storage, structural support.

### 2. Lipids
- **NOT true polymers** (no repeating monomer unit). However, triglyceride
  and phospholipid **assembly still uses dehydration synthesis** — each
  fatty acid –COOH condenses with a glycerol –OH, releasing one H₂O per bond.
- **Triglycerides** (fats/oils): glycerol + 3 fatty acids (3 H₂O released).
  - **Saturated fatty acids**: no C=C double bonds; straight tails pack
    tightly; **tend to be solid at room temperature** (most animal fats).
  - **Unsaturated fatty acids**: ≥1 C=C double bond → **kinks** prevent
    close packing → **tend to be liquid at room temperature** (most plant
    oils). The kink, not the double bond itself, is what lowers the
    melting point.
- **Phospholipids**: glycerol + 2 fatty acids + phosphate head.
  **Amphipathic**: hydrophilic phosphate **head** + hydrophobic two-fatty-acid
  **tails**. In water, heads face outward and tails face inward → forms the
  **lipid bilayer** of every cell membrane. Drives compartmentalization in
  Unit 2.
- **Steroids**: 4 fused carbon rings (e.g., cholesterol, testosterone,
  estrogen).
- **Function**: long-term energy storage, membrane structure, hormones,
  insulation.

### 3. Proteins
- **Monomer**: amino acid (20 standard amino acids).
- Amino acid structure: central carbon (α-carbon) + amino group (–NH₂) +
  carboxyl group (–COOH) + hydrogen + R-group (side chain that determines
  properties).
- **Peptide bonds** link amino acids (formed by dehydration synthesis between
  carboxyl of one and amino of next).
- **Levels of structure**:
  1. **Primary (1°)**: linear sequence of amino acids, held by **peptide
     (covalent) bonds**.
  2. **Secondary (2°)**: α-helices and β-pleated sheets, held by **H-bonds
     in the backbone** (not R-groups).
  3. **Tertiary (3°)**: overall 3D folding driven by R-group interactions —
     H-bonds, ionic, **disulfide bridges** (covalent –S–S– between cysteines),
     and the **hydrophobic effect** (nonpolar R-groups cluster in the interior
     because water excludes them; this is not a true "bond" but a
     thermodynamic exclusion).
  4. **Quaternary (4°)**: multiple polypeptide subunits assembled together
     (e.g., hemoglobin = 4 subunits).
- **Denaturation**: heat, pH, or salt disrupts the **non-covalent** 2°/3°/4°
  interactions (and can reduce disulfides), so the 3D shape unfolds and
  function is lost — but the **primary sequence (peptide bonds) is NOT
  broken**. Renaturation is sometimes possible.
- **Function**: proteins serve as enzymes (e.g., amylase), structural support
  (e.g., collagen in connective tissue, keratin in hair), transport (e.g.,
  hemoglobin carries O₂), defense (antibodies bind antigens), cell signaling
  (receptors, hormones), and motion (motor proteins like myosin and actin).
  These functions are explicitly cited in the AP Bio CED and OpenStax 3.4.

### 4. Nucleic Acids
- **Monomer**: nucleotide — sugar (ribose or deoxyribose) + phosphate +
  nitrogenous base.
- Nucleotides connect via **phosphodiester bonds** (dehydration synthesis
  between the 3′ –OH of one sugar and the 5′ phosphate of the next).
- **Backbone vs bases**: the sugar-phosphate **backbone** runs on the outside;
  the nitrogenous **bases** point inward.
- Two types: **DNA** (deoxyribose, double-stranded, bases A-T-G-C),
  **RNA** (ribose, usually single-stranded, bases A-U-G-C).
- **Directionality and antiparallel strands**: each strand has a 5′ end and a
  3′ end. In DNA, the two strands are **antiparallel** — one runs 5′→3′ while
  its partner runs 3′→5′.
- **Base pairing**: A pairs with T (or U in RNA); G pairs with C.
  - A=T (or A=U) → 2 hydrogen bonds
  - G≡C → 3 hydrogen bonds
  - GC-rich regions are more stable.
- **Function**: store and transmit genetic information. **ATP** is also a
  nucleotide and serves as the cell's energy currency.

## Common AP Bio Misconceptions / Traps for M1

1. **Hydrogen bonds are not "shared electrons"** — they're weak electrostatic
   attractions, not true covalent bonds. Students often confuse this.
2. **Lipids are not technically polymers** — they're not made of repeating
   monomer units. Don't say "lipid polymer."
3. **Water is denser than ice** — ice is LESS dense. The hydrogen-bond
   crystal lattice has more space.
4. **Cohesion ≠ adhesion** — cohesion is water-to-water; adhesion is water-to-
   other-surface.
5. **Cellulose vs starch** — same monomer (glucose) but different bond
   orientation (α vs β); that's why we can digest starch but not cellulose.
6. **Saturated vs unsaturated** — students confuse with kink direction.
   Unsaturated = has C=C double bonds = kink = liquid at room temp.
7. **Dehydration synthesis vs hydrolysis** — students forget which adds and
   which removes water. Trick: **dehydration "removes" (de-) water.**
8. **Primary vs secondary structure** — primary is the SEQUENCE; secondary is
   localized backbone folding (α-helix / β-sheet).
9. **Functional groups change properties** — the same carbon skeleton can be
   very different molecules depending on which groups are attached.
