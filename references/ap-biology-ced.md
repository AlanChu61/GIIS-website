# Unit 2 — Cell Structure and Function

## Prokaryotes vs Eukaryotes

| Feature | Prokaryote (Bacteria, Archaea) | Eukaryote (Protists, Plants, Fungi, Animals) |
|---|---|---|
| Membrane-bound nucleus | No (nucleoid region) | Yes |
| Membrane-bound organelles | No | Yes |
| Typical size | 0.1–5 μm | 10–100 μm |
| DNA shape | Circular, single chromosome | Linear, multiple chromosomes |
| Ribosomes | 70S (smaller) | 80S in cytoplasm; 70S in mitochondria/chloroplasts |
| Cell wall | Peptidoglycan (Bacteria); pseudopeptidoglycan (Archaea) | Cellulose (plants); chitin (fungi); none (animals) |

**Surface area-to-volume ratio**: As a cell grows, volume increases faster than
surface area (V ∝ r³, SA ∝ r²). Small cells have higher SA:V, so diffusion across
membrane meets metabolic needs. Large cells overcome this with folded membranes,
flat shapes, or compartmentalization (organelles).

## Eukaryotic Organelles (functions)

- **Nucleus** — houses DNA; double membrane (nuclear envelope) with nuclear pores.
  Contains the **nucleolus**, site of ribosomal RNA (rRNA) synthesis and ribosome
  subunit assembly.
- **Ribosomes** — protein synthesis; free in cytosol (proteins for the cell)
  or bound to rough ER (proteins for membranes/secretion).
- **Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum (RER)** — studded with ribosomes; synthesizes
  and folds membrane and secreted proteins; entry point to the endomembrane
  system.
- **Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum (SER)** — lipid and steroid synthesis,
  carbohydrate metabolism, calcium storage (muscle), detoxification (liver).
- **Golgi apparatus** — modifies, sorts, packages proteins from RER into
  vesicles; cis face (receives from RER) → trans face (ships out).
- **Lysosomes** — acidic (pH ~5) digestive organelles containing hydrolytic
  enzymes; recycle worn organelles, digest engulfed material (animals).
- **Vacuoles** — storage; central vacuole in plants maintains turgor pressure.
- **Mitochondria** — site of cellular respiration (ATP production via oxidative
  phosphorylation); double membrane (smooth outer, folded inner = cristae);
  contain their own circular DNA and 70S ribosomes (endosymbiotic origin).
- **Chloroplasts** — photosynthesis (plants, algae); double membrane plus
  internal **thylakoid** membranes stacked into **grana**; contain their own
  circular DNA and 70S ribosomes (endosymbiotic origin).
- **Peroxisomes** — break down fatty acids and detoxify; produce and decompose
  H₂O₂ (catalase).
- **Cytoskeleton** — three filament types:
  - **Microfilaments** (actin) — cell shape, muscle contraction, cytokinesis.
  - **Intermediate filaments** — mechanical strength (keratin in skin).
  - **Microtubules** (tubulin) — tracks for motor proteins (kinesin, dynein),
    chromosome separation in mitosis (spindle), cilia, flagella.
- **Centrosomes / centrioles** — microtubule organizing center (animal cells).
- **Cell wall** — outside the plasma membrane; structural support; cellulose
  in plants, chitin in fungi.

## Endosymbiotic Theory

**Mitochondria and chloroplasts evolved from free-living prokaryotes engulfed
by a larger host cell** (~1.5–2 billion years ago). Evidence:
1. Both have a **double membrane** (inner = ancestral prokaryote; outer = host vesicle).
2. Both contain their own **circular DNA** (like bacteria).
3. Both replicate via **binary fission**, independent of the host cell cycle.
4. Both contain **70S ribosomes**, the prokaryote-sized ribosome.

## Endomembrane System

Nuclear envelope ↔ RER ↔ Golgi ↔ vesicles ↔ plasma membrane / lysosomes.
Proteins synthesized at RER are co-translationally inserted into the lumen, vesiculated
to Golgi for modification, then sorted to final destinations.

# Unit 3+4 — Membrane Transport (M3 focus)

## Fluid Mosaic Model

The plasma membrane is a **phospholipid bilayer** with embedded proteins,
cholesterol, and carbohydrates. Described by Singer & Nicolson (1972).

- **Phospholipid bilayer**: hydrophilic phosphate heads face the aqueous
  cytosol and extracellular fluid; hydrophobic fatty acid tails face inward.
- **Cholesterol** (in animal membranes): modulates fluidity — at high temp,
  reduces fluidity; at low temp, prevents tight packing (keeps membrane fluid).
- **Membrane proteins**:
  - **Integral (transmembrane)** — span the bilayer; channels, carriers, pumps,
    receptors.
  - **Peripheral** — bound to one face; signaling, cytoskeletal anchoring.
- **Glycoproteins / glycolipids** — carbohydrate chains on the extracellular
  face; cell recognition, immune signaling.
- The membrane is **selectively permeable**: small nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂)
  cross freely; small polar uncharged molecules (H₂O, urea) cross slowly;
  large polar or charged species (glucose, ions) require transport proteins.

## Passive Transport (no ATP)

Moves substances **down the concentration gradient** (high → low).

- **Simple diffusion** — small nonpolar molecules cross the bilayer directly
  (O₂, CO₂, steroid hormones).
- **Osmosis** — diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane,
  from high water potential (low solute) to low water potential (high solute).
  - **Hypotonic** environment (less solute outside) → water moves IN → cell
    swells/lyses (animal) or becomes turgid (plant; cell wall prevents lysis).
  - **Hypertonic** environment (more solute outside) → water moves OUT → cell
    shrivels (crenation in animals; plasmolysis in plants).
  - **Isotonic** — no net water movement.
- **Facilitated diffusion** — transport via integral proteins, no ATP:
  - **Channel proteins** — hydrophilic tunnels; ion channels (Na⁺, K⁺,
    aquaporins for water).
  - **Carrier proteins** — bind and change conformation (e.g., GLUT1 for
    glucose).

## Active Transport (uses ATP)

Moves substances **against the concentration gradient** (low → high).

- **Primary active transport** — directly hydrolyzes ATP. Example:
  **Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase** pumps 3 Na⁺ out / 2 K⁺ in per ATP, creating the
  electrochemical gradient used by nerve cells.
- **Secondary active transport (cotransport)** — uses the gradient set up by
  primary transport. Example: Na⁺-glucose symporter in intestinal epithelium
  uses Na⁺ flow into the cell to drag glucose in against its gradient.
- **Bulk transport (uses ATP, no transporter needed)**:
  - **Endocytosis** — membrane engulfs material into a vesicle: phagocytosis
    (large particles), pinocytosis (fluid), receptor-mediated (specific ligands).
  - **Exocytosis** — vesicle fuses with plasma membrane, releases contents
    (neurotransmitters, hormones, mucus).

## Common AP traps (M3)

- "Facilitated diffusion uses energy" → **false**. It's passive; it uses
  proteins but not ATP.
- "Osmosis moves solute" → **false**. Osmosis is water movement; solute is
  what creates the gradient.
- "Hypertonic = high water" → **false**. Hypertonic = high solute, LOW water.
- Plants in hypotonic solution don't lyse — the cell wall prevents it; they
  become turgid (healthy).

# Unit 4 — Cell Communication (M4)

## Stages of Cell Signaling (Sutherland)

1. **Reception** — signal molecule (ligand) binds to a receptor protein.
2. **Transduction** — receptor activates a signaling cascade inside the cell.
3. **Response** — cell behavior changes (gene expression, enzyme activation,
   cytoskeletal change).

## Signal Types

- **Direct contact** — gap junctions (animals), plasmodesmata (plants);
  cell-cell recognition via surface proteins.
- **Local signaling** — **paracrine** (neighboring cells, e.g., growth factors);
  **synaptic** (across nerve synapse).
- **Long-distance signaling** — **endocrine** (hormones via bloodstream);
  **neuroendocrine** (combines nerve + hormone).

## Receptor Types

- **G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs)** — 7-transmembrane proteins; ligand
  binding activates an associated G protein (swaps GDP for GTP), which then
  activates downstream effectors (e.g., adenylyl cyclase → cAMP).
- **Receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs)** — single-pass receptors that dimerize
  upon ligand binding; each monomer phosphorylates tyrosines on its partner;
  activates multiple pathways (e.g., insulin receptor, growth factor receptors).
- **Ligand-gated ion channels** — channel opens upon ligand binding (e.g.,
  acetylcholine receptor at neuromuscular junction).
- **Intracellular receptors** — for nonpolar ligands that cross the membrane
  (steroid hormones, thyroid hormone, NO). Ligand-receptor complex enters the
  nucleus and acts as a transcription factor.

## Signal Transduction Cascades

- **Phosphorylation cascade** — kinases sequentially activate one another by
  adding phosphate groups (from ATP); each step **amplifies** the signal
  (one receptor → thousands of activated downstream molecules).
- **Phosphatases** turn the signal off by removing phosphates.
- **Second messengers** — small molecules that diffuse intracellularly:
  - **cAMP** (cyclic AMP) — from ATP via adenylyl cyclase (GPCR pathway).
  - **Ca²⁺** — released from ER stores via IP₃; activates many enzymes.
  - **IP₃ and DAG** — derived from membrane lipid PIP₂.

## Apoptosis

Programmed cell death triggered by internal or external signals (e.g., DNA
damage, developmental cues, immune signals). Executes via **caspase**
proteases that cleave cellular components. Distinguished from necrosis: cell
shrinks, fragments into membrane-bound apoptotic bodies → phagocytosed
without triggering inflammation.

## Quorum Sensing

Bacterial cells release small signaling molecules (**autoinducers**); when
concentration is high enough (high cell density), gene expression changes
(biofilm formation, bioluminescence in *Vibrio fischeri*, virulence factor
production).

# Unit 4 — Cell Cycle and Division (M5)

## The Cell Cycle

```
Interphase (G1 → S → G2)  →  Mitotic phase (M = mitosis + cytokinesis)
```

- **G1 (gap 1)** — cell growth, normal metabolism, organelle duplication.
- **S (synthesis)** — DNA replication; each chromosome now has 2 sister chromatids.
- **G2 (gap 2)** — final growth, prepares for mitosis (microtubules, centrosomes).
- **M phase** — mitosis + cytokinesis.

Cells that exit the cycle enter **G0** (resting, non-dividing — most neurons,
mature muscle cells).

## Mitosis (5 phases)

1. **Prophase** — chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes; nucleolus
   disappears; mitotic spindle forms from centrosomes.
2. **Prometaphase** — nuclear envelope breaks down; spindle microtubules attach
   to chromosomes at **kinetochores**.
3. **Metaphase** — chromosomes align at the **metaphase plate** (cell equator).
4. **Anaphase** — sister chromatids separate (cohesin proteins cleaved by
   separase); pulled to opposite poles.
5. **Telophase** — chromosomes decondense; nuclear envelopes reform; spindle
   disassembles.

**Cytokinesis** — division of the cytoplasm:
- Animal cells: **cleavage furrow** (actin-myosin contractile ring pinches the cell).
- Plant cells: **cell plate** forms from vesicles down the middle (cell wall
  prevents furrowing).

Result: 2 genetically identical diploid daughter cells.

## Checkpoints (regulation)

- **G1 checkpoint ("restriction point")** — most important; cell decides to
  divide or enter G0. Requires DNA integrity, adequate cell size, growth signals.
- **G2 checkpoint** — verifies DNA replication completed correctly.
- **M (spindle) checkpoint** — verifies all kinetochores attached before
  anaphase begins.

Regulated by **cyclins** (concentrations rise and fall through the cycle) and
**cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs)**. Cyclin binding activates CDK; the complex
phosphorylates target proteins that drive the cell into the next phase.
**MPF (M-phase Promoting Factor)** = cyclin B + CDK1; triggers entry into mitosis.

## Cancer = Loss of Cell-Cycle Control

- **Proto-oncogenes** — normal genes that promote cell division (e.g., *Ras*,
  *Myc*); when mutated to **oncogenes** (gain-of-function), they over-promote
  division. Dominant at the cellular level (one mutated copy is enough).
- **Tumor suppressor genes** — normally restrain division or trigger apoptosis
  (e.g., *p53*, *Rb*); loss-of-function mutations (need both alleles knocked
  out) remove the brakes.
- **p53** — "guardian of the genome." Detects DNA damage at G1 checkpoint;
  pauses cycle, triggers DNA repair, or initiates apoptosis. Mutated in >50%
  of human cancers.
- Cancer cells exhibit: uncontrolled division, loss of contact inhibition,
  loss of anchorage dependence, ability to metastasize.

# Unit 5 — Heredity (M6)

## Mendel's Laws

- **Law of Segregation** — each parent has 2 alleles per gene; gametes carry
  only 1 (alleles separate during meiosis I).
- **Law of Independent Assortment** — alleles for different genes sort
  independently when they're on **different chromosomes** (or far apart on
  the same chromosome). Linked genes violate this.
- **Law of Dominance** — in a heterozygote (Aa), the dominant allele's
  phenotype is expressed; the recessive (a) is masked.

## Mendelian Crosses

- **Monohybrid cross** (Aa × Aa) → 3:1 phenotypic ratio, 1:2:1 genotypic ratio.
- **Dihybrid cross** (AaBb × AaBb) → **9:3:3:1** phenotypic ratio (when both
  genes are unlinked).
- **Test cross** — crossing an unknown genotype (dominant phenotype) with
  homozygous recessive (aa) to reveal the unknown's genotype.

## Non-Mendelian Inheritance

- **Incomplete dominance** — heterozygote shows a blended/intermediate
  phenotype (red × white snapdragons → pink). Ratio 1:2:1 (no 3:1 because
  heterozygote is distinct).
- **Codominance** — both alleles fully expressed in heterozygote (ABO blood
  type; AB type expresses both A and B antigens).
- **Multiple alleles** — more than 2 alleles for a gene in the population
  (ABO has Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i).
- **Pleiotropy** — one gene affects many traits (sickle-cell allele affects
  hemoglobin, anemia, organ damage, malaria resistance).
- **Polygenic inheritance** — many genes contribute to one trait (height,
  skin color); produces continuous distributions.
- **Epistasis** — one gene's expression depends on another (coat color in
  Labradors: B/b for color, E/e for whether color shows at all).
- **Sex-linked inheritance** — genes on X chromosome (rare on Y). Males
  (XY) are **hemizygous** — one X, so recessive X-linked alleles always
  expressed. Examples: color blindness, hemophilia, Duchenne muscular dystrophy.

## Meiosis

Two divisions; produces 4 haploid gametes from 1 diploid germ cell.

- **Meiosis I** (reductional) — homologous chromosomes pair (synapsis) and
  separate. **Crossing over** at chiasmata (Prophase I) exchanges DNA between
  non-sister chromatids → genetic recombination.
- **Meiosis II** (equational) — sister chromatids separate (like mitosis on
  haploid cells).

Sources of genetic variation in meiosis:
1. **Crossing over** (Prophase I) — new combinations along chromosomes.
2. **Independent assortment** of homologs (Metaphase I) — 2²³ ≈ 8.4 million
   combinations in humans.
3. **Random fertilization** — combining 2 random gametes.

## Linkage and Recombination

Genes physically close on the same chromosome are **linked**: they tend to
be inherited together (violate independent assortment). Recombination
frequency (% recombinant offspring) measures distance — 1% recombination = 1
centimorgan. Used to build genetic linkage maps.

## Chromosomal Disorders

- **Nondisjunction** — chromosomes fail to separate in meiosis →
  **aneuploidy** (wrong number).
  - Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome).
  - Trisomy 18 (Edwards).
  - XXY (Klinefelter), XO (Turner).
- **Polyploidy** — extra full sets (common and beneficial in plants;
  usually lethal in animals).
- **Chromosomal mutations** — deletion, duplication, inversion, translocation.

# Unit 6 — DNA Structure & Replication (M7)

## DNA Structure (Watson & Crick, 1953; Franklin's X-ray data)

- **Double helix** — two antiparallel strands.
- **Backbone** — alternating sugar (deoxyribose) and phosphate.
- **Bases** — A, T, G, C; A pairs with T (2 H-bonds); G pairs with C (3 H-bonds).
- **Antiparallel** — one strand runs 5'→3'; the complement runs 3'→5'.
- **Major and minor grooves** allow proteins to read sequence without unwinding.

## Chargaff's Rules

In any double-stranded DNA: %A = %T and %G = %C; %purines (A+G) = %pyrimidines
(T+C). This was a key clue to base-pairing before structure was known.

## DNA Replication (semiconservative)

Meselson & Stahl (1958) showed replication is **semiconservative**: each
daughter molecule has one parental strand and one new strand.

Steps and enzymes (E. coli is the canonical example; eukaryotic version
similar but more proteins):

1. **Helicase** unwinds the double helix at the replication fork.
2. **Single-strand binding proteins (SSBPs)** keep strands apart.
3. **Topoisomerase** relieves supercoiling ahead of the fork.
4. **Primase** lays down a short RNA primer (DNA polymerase can't start
   synthesis from scratch — needs a 3' OH).
5. **DNA polymerase III** (in bacteria) / **Pol δ and ε** (eukaryotes) add
   nucleotides 5'→3', complementary to the template.
6. Replication is **continuous on the leading strand** (toward the fork)
   but **discontinuous on the lagging strand** — synthesized in short
   **Okazaki fragments** away from the fork.
7. **DNA polymerase I** (in bacteria) removes RNA primers and replaces with
   DNA.
8. **DNA ligase** seals nicks between Okazaki fragments by forming the
   final phosphodiester bond.

## Proofreading and Repair

- DNA polymerases have **3'→5' exonuclease proofreading** activity (~1
  error per 10⁹ bases after proofreading).
- **Mismatch repair** corrects errors that escape proofreading.
- **Nucleotide excision repair** removes UV-damaged bases (defective in
  xeroderma pigmentosum patients).

## Telomeres

Linear chromosome ends; consist of repetitive sequences (TTAGGG in humans).
Shorten with each replication because the lagging strand can't fully
complete its end (the **end replication problem**). **Telomerase** (active
in germ cells, stem cells, most cancers) extends telomeres using an RNA
template.

# Unit 6 — Gene Expression (M8)

## Central Dogma (Crick)

**DNA → RNA → Protein**. Information flows from DNA via **transcription**
(RNA polymerase) to mRNA, then via **translation** (ribosome + tRNAs) to
protein. Reverse transcriptases (in retroviruses) allow RNA → DNA, an
exception.

## Transcription (eukaryotic)

1. **Initiation** — RNA polymerase II binds to the **promoter** (TATA box
   ~25 bp upstream) with help from general transcription factors.
2. **Elongation** — RNA polymerase reads the template strand 3'→5' and
   synthesizes mRNA 5'→3'. Uses U instead of T.
3. **Termination** — at the terminator sequence, the transcript is released.

## mRNA Processing (eukaryotes only)

The primary transcript (pre-mRNA) is modified before leaving the nucleus:

- **5' cap** (modified guanine) — protects from degradation; ribosome
  recognition.
- **3' poly-A tail** (~200 adenines) — protects from degradation; aids
  export and ribosome recruitment.
- **Splicing** — **spliceosome** (complex of snRNPs) removes **introns**
  and joins **exons**. **Alternative splicing** lets one gene encode
  multiple protein isoforms.

Prokaryotes skip all of this — no nucleus, transcription and translation are
coupled; no introns in protein-coding genes.

## Translation

- **Ribosome** — large (60S) + small (40S) subunits = 80S in eukaryotes;
  has **A site** (incoming aminoacyl-tRNA), **P site** (peptidyl-tRNA), and
  **E site** (exit).
- **tRNAs** carry amino acids; **anticodons** pair with mRNA **codons**
  (3 bases). 64 codons; 61 code for amino acids, 3 are stop codons
  (UAA, UAG, UGA). **AUG** = start codon = methionine.
- **Genetic code** is redundant (multiple codons → same amino acid) and
  nearly universal across life.
- **Steps**: **Initiation** — small ribosomal subunit binds 5' cap, scans
  to AUG, large subunit joins. **Elongation** — codon-anticodon match
  brings aa-tRNA to A site; peptide bond forms (catalyzed by rRNA — a
  **ribozyme**); ribosome translocates one codon; tRNA moves A→P→E.
  **Termination** — stop codon → release factor → polypeptide released.

## Gene Regulation

**Prokaryotes** — operons cluster genes under one promoter and operator:
- ***lac* operon** — inducible. Lactose (allolactose) inactivates the
  repressor → operon turned ON when lactose present. cAMP-CAP boosts
  transcription when glucose is low.
- ***trp* operon** — repressible. Tryptophan activates the repressor →
  operon turned OFF when trp is abundant.

**Eukaryotes** — multi-layered:
- **Chromatin remodeling / epigenetics** — DNA methylation (CpG islands,
  usually represses transcription); histone acetylation (loosens chromatin,
  activates transcription).
- **Transcription factors** bind enhancers/silencers; mediator complex
  bridges to RNA polymerase.
- **Alternative splicing** — different exon combinations.
- **microRNA (miRNA) / RNAi** — short RNAs degrade or block mRNA
  translation.
- **Protein degradation** (ubiquitin-proteasome system).

## Mutations

- **Point mutations** —
  - **Silent** — codon still codes for same amino acid (genetic code redundancy).
  - **Missense** — codes for a different amino acid (e.g., sickle-cell:
    GAG → GTG; glutamate → valine at β-globin position 6).
  - **Nonsense** — codes for a stop codon (premature termination).
- **Frameshift mutations** — insertions or deletions of bases that aren't
  multiples of 3 → shifts the reading frame downstream → usually severely
  damaged protein.
- **Chromosomal mutations** (above, M6).

Mutations are the **ultimate source of new genetic variation** for evolution.

# Unit 6 — Biotechnology (M9)

## Core Tools

- **Restriction enzymes** — bacterial enzymes that cut DNA at specific
  recognition sequences (e.g., **EcoRI** cuts GAATTC). Many produce
  "sticky ends" — short single-stranded overhangs that base-pair with
  matching cuts, enabling recombinant DNA assembly.
- **DNA ligase** — joins cut DNA fragments (phosphodiester bond).
- **Plasmids** — small circular DNA in bacteria, used as cloning vectors;
  often carry an antibiotic-resistance gene for selection.
- **Gel electrophoresis** — separates DNA fragments by **size**. DNA is
  negatively charged (phosphate backbone) → migrates toward positive
  electrode; smaller fragments move faster through agarose gel.
- **PCR (polymerase chain reaction)** — Kary Mullis (1985), Nobel 1993.
  Amplifies a specific DNA region exponentially via repeated cycles of:
  1. **Denaturation** (~95 °C) — strands separate.
  2. **Annealing** (~50–65 °C) — primers bind to template.
  3. **Extension** (~72 °C) — **Taq polymerase** (from heat-stable *Thermus
     aquaticus*) extends primers.
  Each cycle ~doubles target DNA; 30 cycles → ~10⁹-fold amplification.
- **DNA sequencing** — Sanger sequencing uses dideoxynucleotides (ddNTPs)
  that terminate chain elongation; next-generation sequencing (NGS) reads
  millions of fragments in parallel.
- **CRISPR-Cas9** — bacterial defense adapted for genome editing. A
  **guide RNA (gRNA)** directs Cas9 endonuclease to a complementary DNA
  sequence; Cas9 makes a double-strand cut; cell repair pathways either
  knock out the gene or insert a new sequence. Discovered/developed by
  Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier (Nobel 2020).

## Applications

- **Medicine** — recombinant insulin (first approved 1982), gene therapy
  (e.g., SCID), CRISPR trials (sickle-cell, beta-thalassemia, blindness).
- **Agriculture** — genetically modified crops (Bt corn, Roundup-Ready
  soybeans, Golden Rice).
- **Forensics** — DNA fingerprinting from STR markers; CODIS database.
- **Diagnostics** — PCR-based pathogen tests (HIV, COVID-19); prenatal
  screening.

## Ethical considerations

- Germline editing in humans (heritable changes) raises issues of consent
  for future generations, equity of access, unintended consequences.
- Patent law on living organisms / genes (e.g., Myriad Genetics BRCA case).
- Ecological risks of GMO release.
- Privacy of genetic data.

# Unit 7 — Natural Selection and Evolution (M10)

## Darwin's Postulates

1. **Variation** exists in populations.
2. Variation is at least partly **heritable**.
3. More offspring are produced than the environment can support
   (Malthusian struggle).
4. Individuals with traits better suited to the environment have higher
   **differential reproductive success** (fitness) → those traits become
   more common over generations.

Evidence supporting evolution: **fossil record**, **biogeography** (similar
environments → convergent traits, e.g., placental wolf vs marsupial wolf),
**comparative anatomy** (homologous structures from common ancestor,
analogous structures from convergent evolution, vestigial structures),
**comparative embryology**, **molecular sequence similarity**, **direct
observation** (antibiotic resistance, peppered moths, Galápagos finch
beaks).

## Types of Natural Selection (on continuous traits)

- **Directional selection** — favors one extreme; trait mean shifts
  (e.g., peppered moths during Industrial Revolution).
- **Stabilizing selection** — favors the mean; reduces variation (e.g.,
  human birth weight).
- **Disruptive selection** — favors both extremes against the middle; can
  drive sympatric speciation (e.g., African seedcrackers' beak sizes).
- **Sexual selection** — competition for mates; produces sexual dimorphism
  (peacock tails, deer antlers).

## Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

A population is in H-W equilibrium when allele frequencies do NOT change
generation to generation — the "null model" for evolution.

Conditions (all 5 must hold; failure of any = evolution):
1. No mutation.
2. No migration (gene flow).
3. Random mating (no sexual selection).
4. Very large population (no genetic drift).
5. No natural selection.

Equations: if p = frequency of allele A and q = frequency of a, then
**p + q = 1**, and genotype frequencies are
**p² + 2pq + q² = 1** (AA + Aa + aa).

## Mechanisms of Evolution (causes H-W to fail)

- **Mutation** — ultimate source of new alleles; rare per generation.
- **Gene flow** — allele migration between populations; tends to make
  populations more similar.
- **Genetic drift** — random change in allele frequencies, strongest in
  small populations:
  - **Bottleneck effect** — population crash → reduced variation (e.g.,
    cheetahs, northern elephant seals).
  - **Founder effect** — small group colonizes a new area; their alleles
    dominate (e.g., Amish populations, polydactyly).
- **Natural selection** — non-random; favors higher-fitness alleles.
- **Non-random mating** — assortative mating, inbreeding; changes
  genotype frequencies (e.g., more homozygotes) but not allele frequencies
  directly.

## Fitness

**Fitness** = relative reproductive success (number of offspring that
themselves survive to reproduce). Not "strength" or "longevity." High
fitness is **context-dependent** — depends on the environment.

## Coevolution

Two species exert reciprocal selective pressures (predator-prey arms
races, mutualisms like flower-pollinator, host-parasite).

# Unit 7 — Speciation and Phylogeny (M11)

## Species Concepts

- **Biological species concept (Mayr)** — a group of populations whose
  members can interbreed and produce **fertile** offspring; reproductively
  isolated from other groups. Limits: doesn't apply to asexual organisms,
  fossils, or many bacteria.
- **Morphological species concept** — based on physical traits.
- **Ecological species concept** — based on niche.
- **Phylogenetic species concept** — based on shared evolutionary
  ancestry.

## Reproductive Isolating Mechanisms

**Prezygotic** (prevent fertilization):
- **Habitat isolation** — different habitats within an area.
- **Temporal isolation** — different breeding times/seasons.
- **Behavioral isolation** — different courtship rituals (firefly flashes).
- **Mechanical isolation** — incompatible anatomy.
- **Gametic isolation** — sperm/egg can't fuse (different surface proteins).

**Postzygotic** (after fertilization):
- **Reduced hybrid viability** — hybrid embryo doesn't develop.
- **Reduced hybrid fertility** — hybrid develops but is sterile (mule:
  horse × donkey).
- **Hybrid breakdown** — F1 OK, but F2 weak or sterile.

## Modes of Speciation

- **Allopatric speciation** — geographic barrier physically separates
  populations → independent evolution → reproductive isolation
  (Death Valley pupfish, Galápagos finches).
- **Sympatric speciation** — speciation without geographic separation:
  - **Polyploidy** (especially in plants — common: wheat is hexaploid).
  - **Habitat differentiation** within a region.
  - **Sexual selection** (cichlid fish in African lakes).
- **Parapatric speciation** — partially overlapping ranges; selection
  pressure varies across a gradient.

## Pace of Speciation

- **Gradualism** — slow accumulation of changes over long time.
- **Punctuated equilibrium (Eldredge & Gould)** — long periods of
  stasis, rare bursts of rapid speciation.

Both patterns occur; fossil record shows both.

## Phylogenetic Trees and Cladistics

A **phylogenetic tree** (or **cladogram**) represents evolutionary
relationships. **Nodes** = common ancestors; **branches** = lineages;
**tips** = extant or extinct taxa.

- **Clade** = an ancestor and ALL its descendants (monophyletic group).
- **Paraphyletic** = an ancestor and SOME descendants (e.g., "reptiles"
  if birds excluded).
- **Polyphyletic** = members from different ancestors (e.g., "warm-blooded
  animals" — mammals and birds independently).
- **Synapomorphy** = shared derived trait that defines a clade.
- **Plesiomorphy** = ancestral (shared with outgroup) trait.

Trees can be inferred from morphology and increasingly from molecular
data (DNA/protein sequences). **Molecular clocks** estimate divergence
times from the rate of neutral mutations.

# Unit 8 — Population Ecology (M12)

## Population Parameters

- **Population size (N)** — total individuals.
- **Population density** — individuals per area/volume.
- **Dispersion patterns** — **clumped** (most common; resources patchy),
  **uniform** (territorial), **random** (rare).
- **Demography** — birth (b), death (d), immigration (i), emigration (e).
- **Per-capita growth rate**: r = (b − d) + (i − e).

## Growth Models

**Exponential growth (J-curve)** — unlimited resources:
**dN/dt = rN**. Population doubles at a constant interval (doubling time
≈ 70/r). Rare in nature; seen in early colonization or after disturbance.

**Logistic growth (S-curve)** — limited by **carrying capacity (K)**:
**dN/dt = rN × (K − N) / K**. Growth slows as N approaches K; population
levels off at K.

## Limiting Factors

- **Density-dependent** — intensify as N grows: competition, disease,
  predation, accumulation of waste, territoriality.
- **Density-independent** — affect populations regardless of size:
  weather extremes, fire, floods, volcanic eruptions, human disturbance.

## Life History Strategies

- **r-selected** — many offspring, little parental care, short lifespan,
  early reproduction (insects, weeds, many fish).
- **K-selected** — few offspring, heavy parental care, long lifespan,
  late reproduction (humans, elephants, large mammals).

Most species lie on a spectrum, not at extremes.

## Survivorship Curves

- **Type I** — high survival in early/middle life, mortality late (humans,
  large mammals — K-selected).
- **Type II** — constant mortality throughout life (some birds, small
  mammals, reptiles).
- **Type III** — high early mortality, few survivors live long (oysters,
  many fish, plants — r-selected).

# Unit 8 — Community Ecology (M13)

## Interspecific Interactions

| Interaction | Sp. 1 | Sp. 2 | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Competition** | − | − | Two warbler species using same tree (Robert MacArthur) |
| **Predation** | + | − | Lynx/hare |
| **Herbivory** | + | − | Deer/plant |
| **Parasitism** | + | − | Tapeworm/host |
| **Mutualism** | + | + | Coral/zooxanthellae; mycorrhizae/plant roots |
| **Commensalism** | + | 0 | Barnacle on whale |

## Competitive Exclusion (Gause)

Two species competing for the exact same limiting resource can't coexist
indefinitely — one outcompetes the other. **Resource partitioning** lets
similar species coexist by using slightly different niches (e.g.,
MacArthur's warblers each feeding in different parts of the same spruce
tree).

## Niche

- **Fundamental niche** — full set of conditions a species could occupy.
- **Realized niche** — what it actually occupies, after competition and
  other interactions.

## Predator-Prey Dynamics

Lotka-Volterra equations predict cyclical oscillations in predator and
prey populations. Classic empirical example: **Canada lynx and snowshoe
hare** (Hudson's Bay fur records, ~10-year cycles).

Coevolutionary defenses: **cryptic coloration** (camouflage), **aposematic
coloration** (warning, e.g., poison dart frogs), **Batesian mimicry**
(harmless mimics harmful — viceroy/monarch was reconsidered but the term
applies to many cases), **Müllerian mimicry** (multiple harmful species
share warning patterns), behavioral defenses, chemical defenses.

## Keystone Species

Species whose effect on the community is disproportionately large
relative to its abundance. Classic study: **Robert Paine's removal of
Pisaster ochraceus** (sea star) from intertidal zone → mussel monoculture,
biodiversity collapse. Wolves in Yellowstone: top-down trophic cascade.

## Ecological Succession

Predictable changes in community composition over time after disturbance.

- **Primary succession** — starts on bare rock with no soil (volcanic
  islands, glacial retreat). Pioneers: lichens, mosses. Builds soil over
  centuries.
- **Secondary succession** — disturbance removed vegetation but soil
  remains (forest fire, abandoned farm). Faster than primary; early
  herbaceous → shrubs → forest.

## Species Diversity

- **Species richness** — number of species.
- **Species evenness** — relative abundances.
- **Shannon diversity index (H)** combines both.
- Higher diversity generally → more stable ecosystem (resistance and
  resilience), more productive, more resistant to invasion.

# Unit 8 — Ecosystems and Energy Flow (M14, M15, M16)

## Trophic Levels

1. **Producers** (autotrophs) — fix energy from sunlight (photosynthesis)
   or chemicals (chemosynthesis). Plants, algae, cyanobacteria.
2. **Primary consumers** (herbivores).
3. **Secondary consumers** (carnivores eating herbivores).
4. **Tertiary consumers** (top carnivores).
5. **Decomposers** (bacteria, fungi) — break down dead matter, recycle
   nutrients at every level.

## Energy Flow (One-Way)

- Sun → producers → consumers → decomposers → lost as heat.
- Energy **flows** through the ecosystem; **matter cycles**.
- **10% rule (ecological efficiency)** — roughly 10% of energy at one
  trophic level is incorporated into biomass at the next; the other 90%
  is lost as heat (cellular respiration), undigested material, or unused
  parts. Why food chains rarely have more than 4–5 levels.
- **Gross Primary Productivity (GPP)** = total energy fixed by producers.
- **Net Primary Productivity (NPP)** = GPP − respiration by producers =
  energy available to consumers. Highest in tropical rainforests, coral
  reefs, estuaries; lowest in deserts, deep ocean.

## Ecological Pyramids

- **Pyramid of energy** — always upright (10% rule).
- **Pyramid of biomass** — usually upright; can be inverted in aquatic
  systems (small, fast-reproducing phytoplankton support more zooplankton
  by biomass at any moment).
- **Pyramid of numbers** — can be inverted (one tree supports many
  insects).

## Biogeochemical Cycles

### Carbon Cycle

- Atmospheric CO₂ ↔ producers (photosynthesis fixes C into glucose) ↔
  consumers and decomposers (respiration releases CO₂).
- **Long-term storage**: fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas — buried biomass),
  carbonate rocks (limestone), ocean dissolved CO₂.
- **Human disruption**: burning fossil fuels transfers ancient C to the
  atmosphere → atmospheric CO₂ rose from ~280 ppm (pre-industrial) to
  >420 ppm (2024). Drives **global warming** (greenhouse effect) and
  **ocean acidification** (CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → lower ocean pH).

### Nitrogen Cycle

N₂ (atmosphere, 78%) is inert and unusable by most organisms. Steps:

1. **Nitrogen fixation** — N₂ → NH₃/NH₄⁺. Done by **Rhizobium** bacteria
   in legume root nodules, cyanobacteria, or industrially (Haber-Bosch
   process for fertilizer).
2. **Nitrification** — NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻. By soil bacteria (*Nitrosomonas*,
   *Nitrobacter*).
3. **Assimilation** — plants take up NO₃⁻ or NH₄⁺ and build amino acids
   and nucleic acids. Animals get N by eating.
4. **Ammonification** — decomposers convert organic N back to NH₄⁺.
5. **Denitrification** — anaerobic bacteria convert NO₃⁻ back to N₂ gas.

Human impact: synthetic fertilizers → runoff → **eutrophication** (algal
blooms → decomposition consumes oxygen → **dead zones** in coastal waters
like Gulf of Mexico).

### Water Cycle

Evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation, runoff,
infiltration. Powered by solar energy.

### Phosphorus Cycle

Unlike C and N, no atmospheric component. Cycles slowly via rock
weathering → soil → plants → consumers → back to soil/sediments. Often a
**limiting nutrient** in freshwater and many soils.

## Human Impacts on Ecosystems

- **Climate change** — rising atmospheric greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄,
  N₂O) → warming, sea level rise, range shifts, increased extreme weather,
  ocean acidification, coral bleaching.
- **Habitat destruction** — leading cause of biodiversity loss
  (deforestation, urbanization, agriculture).
- **Pollution** — air, water, plastics; **biomagnification** of toxins
  (DDT, mercury, PCBs) up trophic levels.
- **Invasive species** — disrupt native communities (zebra mussels, kudzu,
  brown tree snakes).
- **Overharvesting** — fisheries collapse (Atlantic cod), megafauna
  extinctions.

## AP Investigation Anchor — Productivity

A common AP investigation: measure NPP in aquatic ecosystems by tracking
dissolved oxygen change in light vs dark bottles (light bottle measures
NPP; dark measures respiration; difference + dark = GPP).

- **Section I — 60 multiple-choice questions, 90 min (50% of score)**.
- **Section II — 6 free-response questions, 90 min (50%)**:
  - 2 long FRQs (each 8–10 points) — interpret an experiment.
  - 4 short FRQs (each 4 points) — analyze data, model, predict outcomes.

  1. Concept explanation, 2. Visual representation, 3. Question/method,
